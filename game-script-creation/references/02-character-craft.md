## 1. Character Core: Want vs. Need, the Ghost, the Lie

### The Framework (Weiland + Truby synthesis)

Every compelling character is built on a hidden fracture. Two complementary frameworks explain it:

**K.M. Weiland's Lie/Need/Want triangle** (from *Creating Character Arcs*, 2016):
- **The Wound (Ghost):** A past event — usually backstory — that broke the character's trust in something. Not just "bad thing happened" but the specific psychological damage it caused.
- **The Lie:** The false belief the character built around that wound. Not consciously chosen — it's the story they tell themselves to survive. Example: a character abandoned in childhood believes "I don't deserve love" or "trusting people leads to pain." The Lie warps every decision.
- **The Want:** The external goal. What the character *thinks* will fix them — the object, person, status, or revenge they're chasing. It's the symptom cure, not the disease cure. In gacha terms: the reason they pick up the sword, join the faction, or pursue the MacGuffin.
- **The Need:** The internal truth the character must accept to actually heal. Almost always the *opposite* of the Lie. The story is the pressure system that forces the character toward it — or breaks them trying.

**The key diagnostic:** If the Want is achieved but the Need isn't, the character is still broken. Great stories make the audience feel this distinction long before the character does.

**John Truby's parallel model** (from *Anatomy of Story*, 2007):
- **Ghost:** An old wound from the past that still controls the hero in the present. Structural function: it acts as a *counterdesire* — the desire drives the hero forward, the ghost holds them back. This creates organic internal conflict without authorial intervention.
- **Psychological Need:** What the hero must overcome internally to stop hurting themselves.
- **Moral Need:** What the hero must change to stop hurting *others*. Truby distinguishes these — a character can heal psychologically while remaining morally broken (the disillusionment arc), or vice versa.
- **Desire (External Goal):** The surface story engine.

**Lajos Egri's three-dimensional "bone structure"** (from *The Art of Dramatic Writing*, 1946, still foundational):
- Every character must be known in three dimensions simultaneously: **physiological** (body, appearance, heredity), **sociological** (class, education, occupation, community), and **psychological** (moral standards, ambition, fears, temperament, internal contradictions).
- Egri's critical insight: characters without all three dimensions are puppets. The psychological dimension is where the *will* lives — and will (not plot) drives drama. A character's will clashing against another character's will IS the story.

### Defining the Core Trait in One Line

Technique: write the character's essence as *"X was the kind of person who ___"*. Fill the blank with a behavior, not an adjective. "He was the kind of person who read the terms of service for fun" tells you his paranoid precision better than calling him "anxious." The behavior radiates outward: his jokes, his silences, his fights, his blind spots — everything flows from that one truth.

**Concrete example — Kafka (Honkai: Star Rail):**  
Her designers built her around a single inversion of her literary namesake Franz Kafka: where Kafka's world traps the innocent in systems without exit, HSR's Kafka "was born of the web and is never caught by it." Core trait: *freedom through absolute detachment, expressed as predatory warmth*. Every design choice — the insect-segmented jacket sleeves, the title card "Dormancy" referencing "Tonight No One Sleeps" (Puccini opera about love that conquers cold), the companion quest named after Stefan Zweig's "Letter from an Unknown Woman" — radiates from that one contradiction.

**Pitfall:** Defining a core trait as a single adjective ("brave," "cunning") produces flat characters. An adjective is a category; a behavior + contradiction is a person. *"She was the kind of person who laughed loudest at funerals"* is a person.

### How to Apply

1. Identify the wound first. What happened? Be specific — not "she had a hard childhood" but "she watched her mentor sacrifice themselves for a cause she later discovered was manufactured."
2. Derive the Lie from the wound logically. What false belief does that experience produce? ("Self-sacrifice is how love gets weaponized.")
3. Derive the Want from the Lie. What does someone who believes that chase? (Power over others, so no one can weaponize her sacrifice.)
4. Derive the Need as the antithesis. (She needs to learn that love without power is still real.)
5. Write the one-line core trait. ("She was the kind of person who made you feel trusted while she checked all your exits.")

## 2. Character Arcs: Beats and Types

### The Five Arc Types (Weiland framework)

**Positive Change Arc** ("Truth Arc")  
The character moves from Lie → Truth. The external plot is a pressure system that forces confrontation with the wound. Classic heroic structure.

Key beats:
1. *Ghost established (before Act 1)* — the wound exists but isn't named yet.
2. *Inciting event* — disrupts the character's stasis-state built around the Lie.
3. *First glimpse of the truth* — early Act 2, the Need surfaces briefly but is rejected.
4. *Midpoint shift* — character commits to the Want harder, or gets their biggest false victory.
5. *Lie-driven disaster* — the Lie causes maximum damage, usually around the Act 2 break.
6. *Dark night of the soul* — character confronts the wound directly for the first time.
7. *Truth embraced / climax decision* — the final choice is made with the Need's truth, not the Lie.

Famous example: *Zuko (Avatar: The Last Airbender)*. Wound = father's rejection. Lie = "Honor only comes through my father's approval." Want = capture the Avatar. Need = discover that honor is self-defined. His arc takes three full seasons of pressure before the truth lands.

**Flat Arc ("Steadfast Arc")**  
The character already holds the Truth. The story uses them to change the *world around them*, not themselves. They're tested — they may temporarily wobble — but they don't fundamentally change.

When to use: stories where the protagonist represents an ideal (principle, system, worldview) under siege. The external world is "infected" by a Lie, and the hero is the antidote.

Beats: hero exposes false beliefs in supporting characters; their truth is challenged by antagonist forces; supporting characters change (or fail to). Hero is stressed but doesn't break.

Famous example: *Atticus Finch (To Kill a Mockingbird)*. His function is to be unmoved by the Lie the town holds. His "arc" is about proving the truth survives even when it loses.

In gacha context: flat-arc characters make excellent permanent-party NPCs and mentor figures. They anchor the game world's moral center while the player-avatar or rotating cast changes around them.

**Negative Arcs (three subtypes)**  
All three start with a Lie and end without achieving Truth:

| Subtype | What happens | Famous example |
|---|---|---|
| Corruption Arc | Character actively rejects Truth and embraces the Lie | Michael Corleone (*The Godfather*) |
| Disillusionment Arc | Character discovers Truth but it destroys them rather than saving them | Walter White (early seasons — moves toward corruption after) |
| Fall Arc | Character never meaningfully encounters Truth; Lie consumes them | Heathcliff (*Wuthering Heights*) |

When to use: antagonists, cautionary side characters, and — in gacha — limited-event characters who die or defect. The disillusionment arc is particularly powerful for characters who are *right about everything but wrong about people.*

**Beginner pitfall:** Confusing "character has flaws" with "character has an arc." A character can have many flaws and zero arc if none of those flaws are under meaningful pressure during the story. The arc requires that the wound is specifically attacked by the plot.

## 3. Archetypes and Roles

### Vogler's 7 Narrative Archetypes (from *The Writer's Journey*, 1992 — practical refinement of Campbell)

These are *functional roles in a story structure*, not personality types. The same character can occupy multiple roles at different moments:

| Role | Function | Common form | Gacha application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hero** | Experiences journey, learns | Protagonist / player avatar | Main character / 旅行者 / 开拓者 |
| **Mentor** | Provides gifts, training, wisdom | Sage, old master | Veteran faction member, returns at key beats |
| **Threshold Guardian** | Tests hero's commitment | Antagonist, obstacle | Mini-boss / gatekeeper NPC / skeptic ally |
| **Herald** | Announces change | Messenger, inciting figure | The character who brings the inciting event |
| **Shapeshifter** | Creates uncertainty, doubt | Trickster, double agent | Love interest with ambiguous motives; rival |
| **Shadow** | Represents the hero's darkness | Villain, dark mirror | Final antagonist / dark-side version of protagonist |
| **Trickster** | Punctures pretension, creates chaos | Comic relief, provocateur | Support character who breaks tension and forces honesty |

**Critical caution:** Vogler's roles are tools, not cages. Handing a character a single role without individual wound/need/voice produces a function, not a person. The Mentor who *only* mentors is a plot device. Give them a wound that conflicts with their mentoring — then they become a character.

### Carol Pearson's 12 Personality Archetypes (motivation-based)

Note: often mislabeled "Jungian" — Carol Pearson's framework from *Awakening the Heroes Within* (1991) maps motivational drives to 12 types: Innocent, Orphan, Warrior, Caregiver, Seeker, Lover, Destroyer, Creator, Ruler, Magician, Sage, Jester.

Use for: establishing a character's *baseline motivation structure* before adding wounds and complications. An Orphan archetype enters every relationship already expecting abandonment — that's their default cognitive lens. A Ruler archetype defaults to control as the answer to any problem.

**Pitfall:** These types are strong starting axes but become stereotypes without contradiction layered on top.

### Propp's 7 Character Functions (from *Morphology of the Folktale*, 1928)

Vladimir Propp analyzed 100 Russian folk tales and found the same seven character functions appear repeatedly. Characters may combine functions:

1. **Villain** — opposes the hero
2. **Donor** — provides a magical agent or gift after a test
3. **Helper** — assists the hero
4. **Princess (and her father)** — the sought person / reward
5. **Dispatcher** — sends the hero on the quest
6. **Hero** — seeks or reacts to the villain
7. **False Hero** — competes with the protagonist, initially credited

Most gacha games unconsciously follow this structure in their main story: the world organization dispatches you (Dispatcher), senior characters give you tools and training (Donor), companion characters help you (Helper), the endgame antagonist opposes you (Villain), and a limited-story NPC might fill the False Hero role — appearing to solve the problem before being revealed as inadequate or corrupt.

### Enneagram as Character-Building Tool (not horoscope)

The Enneagram's value to writers is not in categorizing types but in understanding **automatic responses to stress** and **core fear vs. core desire** for each type. Each type has a deep fear that shapes every behavior:

| Type | Core Fear | Core Desire | Stress direction (disintegrates toward) | Example character |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (Reformer) | Being bad/wrong | Goodness | Type 4 (dramatic/moody) | Atticus Finch; Erwin Smith (AoT) |
| 2 (Helper) | Being unlovable | Being loved | Type 8 (aggressive/controlling) | Tohru Honda (Fruits Basket) |
| 3 (Achiever) | Being worthless | Having value | Type 9 (disengaged) | Tony Stark (early MCU) |
| 4 (Individualist) | Having no identity | Being unique | Type 2 (over-attached) | Blanche DuBois |
| 5 (Investigator) | Being helpless | Competence | Type 7 (scattered) | Sherlock Holmes; Severus Snape |
| 6 (Loyalist) | Having no support | Certainty | Type 3 (image-driven) | Ron Weasley |
| 7 (Enthusiast) | Being trapped | Freedom | Type 1 (rigid/critical) | Jack Sparrow |
| 8 (Challenger) | Being controlled | Self-reliance | Type 5 (withdrawn) | Magneto; Vegeta |
| 9 (Peacemaker) | Conflict/disconnection | Peace | Type 6 (anxious) | Frodo Baggins |

**How to apply:** Pick a type for a character's *default coping mechanism* (not their whole person). Then write what happens under stress — the type disintegrates toward its stress line. This gives automatic, psychologically consistent behavior in conflict scenes without the author having to invent a response from scratch.

**Example:** A Type 5 character (hoards knowledge, fears incompetence) under severe stress moves toward Type 7 behavior — becomes scattered, impulsive, makes reckless decisions to escape the anxiety. Writing this transition from "controlled withdrawal" to "chaotic action" gives you your Act 2 break beat for free.

**Pitfall:** Using the Enneagram as a horoscope ("she's a 4, so she does X") rather than as a pressure map. The type is the default; stress and growth move the character *away* from it.

## 4. Relationships and Ensemble Dynamics

### Foil vs. Mirror: Two Distinct Tools

**Mirror character:** Shares the protagonist's wound but made *different* choices in response. Creates resonance — the protagonist sees themselves in the mirror character. When the mirror succeeds at the protagonist's journey or fails at it, the protagonist is shown a possible future/past. Not contrast; *amplification.*

**Foil character:** Occupies the same narrative space with similar goals but runs on *different internal logic.* Creates friction — the foil challenges the protagonist's worldview without being wrong. A foil who is simply "the opposite personality" is a prop; a foil who genuinely might be right is a dramatic engine.

**Practical test:** "Is this supporting character forcing the protagonist to reconsider something internally or ideologically?" If no — they're not doing their job.

### Ensemble Pressuring: How Supporting Cast Attacks the Protagonist's Lie

Linda Seger (*Creating Unforgettable Characters*, 1990) identifies that each secondary character must contrast not just with the protagonist but with each other — otherwise the ensemble becomes monotonous even when individually well-drawn.

More usefully: each key supporting character should embody a *different relationship to the protagonist's Lie*:
- One character who **represents the Lie made flesh** (if you keep believing this, this is where you end up)
- One character who **offers the Truth but is initially rejected** (the mentor, the love interest, the honest friend)
- One character who **validates the Lie temporarily** (makes the protagonist feel the Lie is working — deepens the false comfort)
- One character who **is damaged by the protagonist's Lie-driven behavior** (raises the moral stakes)

In gacha games, this ensemble logic applies to the rotating story cast of each chapter. The protagonist (player avatar) has a fixed Lie or worldview being tested. Gacha characters introduced in a given version often map naturally to one of these four supporting functions in relation to that version's theme.

### Want-Conflict Between Characters

Truby's core ensemble principle: give every major character a *want that collides with other characters' wants* — not because they're enemies but because their wants structurally conflict. Two characters who both want to protect the same person may have incompatible methods; two characters who want to be right create debate that illuminates the theme.

## 5. The Character Bible / 人設 Template

A usable character bible is not a census form — it's a *decision engine*. Every field must answer the question: "What does this tell me about how this character will behave when I don't know what to write?"

### Compact Usable Template

```
CHARACTER BIBLE — [Character Name]
=====================================

CORE (the decision engine — fill this first)
  One-line essence:     [X was the kind of person who ___]
  Core Trait:           [One word + the contradiction that makes it interesting]
  Wound/Ghost:          [Specific past event + what it broke]
  The Lie:              [False belief that grew from the wound]
  The Truth (Need):     [What they must accept to heal]
  External Want:        [What they're chasing in THIS story]

VOICE & BEHAVIOR  
  Speech pattern:       [Formal/clipped/verbose/deflecting/uses humor as armor]
  Signature verbal tic: [One recurring phrase, word choice, or pattern]
  How they enter a room: [Do they scan for exits? Assess everyone? Find the food?]
  What they never say directly: [What do they deflect, euphemize, or silence?]
  What makes them laugh: [Real laughter vs. performance laughter]

RELATIONSHIPS
  Closest person (and why that's complicated): 
  Character they most misunderstand:
  Character who sees through them:
  Character they would sacrifice for (and the conditions):

CONTRADICTIONS (make them real)
  What they project:    [The persona they show the world]
  What they actually are: [The self behind the projection]
  Where the gap shows:  [The unguarded moment when the mask slips]

VISUAL / SENSORY MOTIF
  Color/palette:        [Not just aesthetic — what does this color mean for them?]
  Object they always carry or return to:
  Physical tell when lying/scared:

ARC (for this story)
  Arc type:             [Positive / Flat / Negative — specify subtype]
  End state:            [Where do they end? Wiser? Broken? Changed? Unchanged?]

GACHA-SPECIFIC (if applicable)
  "Pull hook" trait:    [The one thing players notice in 3 seconds]
  Gap Moe contradiction: [Surface trait vs. hidden opposite — see Section 6]
  Signature skill/ability concept: [How does their gameplay identity mirror their character?]
  Banner story angle:   [What one question does their story explore?]
```

**Rule:** If you can't fill "One-line essence" without resorting to adjectives, you don't know the character yet. Go back to the wound.

## 6. Character-First Design for Gacha Games (二游 / 二次元手游)

### Why Characters ARE the Product

In 二游, characters carry weight that purely narrative characters don't:
1. **Gameplay unit** — mechanical identity must be legible, distinct, and feel true to personality
2. **Merchandise / IP** — visual design must work on acrylic stands, voice packs, pillow covers
3. **Story driver** — must carry a banner quest or event arc with satisfying internal stakes
4. **Gacha motivation** — must make players *want* to pull, not just because they're strong

These four functions create tension. A character optimized purely for memorability may be story-thin. One optimized for depth may not convert pulls. The craft challenge is making all four vectors point in the same direction.

### The Gap Moe (反差萌) Principle

Gap moe (反差萌) is the systematic use of contradiction to create memorability and affection. The principle: when a character's *actual* behavior sharply contradicts their *apparent* surface presentation, and that gap is in a "beautiful direction" (unexpected softness, hidden competence, surprising warmth, secret awkwardness), it creates the psychological surprise that produces strong emotional attachment.

**Mechanism:** The brain registers surprise, then resolution — "oh, that's who they actually are." The second layer feels *discovered* rather than declared, which produces stronger bonding than stated traits.

**The formula:** `[dominant visible trait] + [sharp interior contradiction] = memorable hook`

Common high-impact gap moe patterns:
- Intimidating/powerful exterior + childlike vulnerability interior (classic tsundere variant)
- Cold/professional surface + hidden intensity of feeling (Kafka in HSR — "predatory warmth")
- Cheerful/lighthearted surface + profound underlying grief (Blue Archive's Sensei relationship with students)
- Ancient/powerful being + genuinely baffled by minor modern things
- Extremely competent at one thing + catastrophically incompetent at something adjacent

**Critical caveat (from research):** *"The greater the distance between elements, the greater the gap moe, but if the gap is too big, it becomes difficult to grasp what kind of character they are."* Gap moe requires a solid core — the contradiction must be two facets of the same person, not two different characters stitched together. The contradiction must be *explainable* by the wound/lie structure: why does this intimidating person become awkward around small animals? Because their wound involved being helpless, and small creatures remind them of their own vulnerability.

### miHoYo's Three-Layer Character Design Method (from GDC 2021 / Zhihu analysis)

From the Genshin Impact GDC 2021 talk and subsequent CN dev analyses, miHoYo's character approach operates on three integrated layers that must be designed simultaneously:

1. **Visual identity** — design must communicate personality in a static pose. Color and element as psychological anchor (ice/blue = calm; lightning/purple = mystery and speed; geo/yellow = solidity). Dynamic lines for active characters, contained lines for reserved ones. Visual motif must survive as a keychain.

2. **Mechanical identity** — gameplay ability must *feel* like the character's personality in motion. Kafka's lightning DoT mechanic — she poisons, waits, lets the damage compound — mirrors her predatory patience. Silver Wolf hacks the game itself, matching her "the universe is a simulation I can edit" worldview. When character personality and gameplay verb align, players feel the character is *real.*

3. **Story identity** — each character carries one question their story explores. Not the character's arc globally, but the banner-scale question: "What does this person teach the player about [theme]?" This makes even 20-minute companion quests feel complete rather than promotional.

### How HSR's Kafka Was Built from Literary Reference

The GCORES analysis (机核, 2024) documents the design process: Kafka's character is built from three literary texts layered into her appearance and quest structure:
- Her banner name 【夜将不眠】references Puccini's "Nessun Dorma" — about love that conquers coldness through persistence
- Her companion quest is titled after Stefan Zweig's *Letter from an Unknown Woman* — one-sided love across power asymmetry
- Her design (spider/insect visual motifs) inverts Franz Kafka's *Metamorphosis* — where Kafka is trapped in transformation, HSR's Kafka transforms others while remaining free

This is not decoration — it is a structured argument about the character's Lie ("love is a cage") expressed simultaneously in gameplay (she chains others), narrative (she's the object of others' obsession), and visual design (she wears the predator's markings without being caught).

**The lesson for non-pro writers:** You don't need literary references of this density. But you do need to ask: "What does every layer of this character — name, visual, mechanical verb, story theme — say about the same thing?" If the layers say different things, the character won't cohere.

### Avoiding the Mary Sue Trap in Gacha

In gacha specifically, Mary Sue risk is high because:
- Characters must be sympathetic enough to pull for
- Commercial incentive rewards likable traits
- Writers may soften flaws to avoid alienating players

Mary Sue diagnostic: Does every character in the scene exist to validate this character? Does the character solve every problem they face without meaningful cost? Are their "flaws" actually strengths in disguise (clumsy, but endearingly!)? Do other characters abandon their own motivations to orbit this one?

The fix: **Give the character a flaw that is *actually a flaw* in the story's context.** Not a flaw that gets forgiven — a flaw that *costs them something.* In gacha, this cost often happens in the character's backstory/companion quest, before the player "gets" them, and the gameplay feels like the character's victory over that old cost.

### Designing a Character Who Carries Their Own Banner Story

A banner story (角色传说任务, 邀约任务) is a short, self-contained narrative. For it to work, the character must:
- Have a **specific question their story poses** (not "learn about this character" — that's a tourism brochure, not a story)
- Have a **status quo disrupted** within the banner's scope
- Show **one genuine vulnerability** not visible in the main story
- End with **a moment of choice or revelation** that the player feels they witnessed — not just were told about

The practical structure: establish the character's surface-persona in the first beat, disrupt it with a situation that can't be handled by the persona, force the character into the gap-moe moment, let the player see the contradiction, and resolve with a quiet truth rather than a proclamation.

## 7. Common Beginner Pitfalls and Corrections

| Pitfall | Why it happens | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Adjective-trait characters ("she's brave and kind") | Character feels complete but behaves the same in every scene | Replace with behavioral pattern + contradiction; if the trait doesn't cost anything, it's decoration |
| Wound without consequences | Backstory tragedy exists but doesn't affect present behavior | The Lie must be *active* — making wrong decisions NOW because of the wound |
| The arc that only happens in backstory | Character tells us they changed; we don't see the change under story pressure | The wound must be attacked by the plot's events, not resolved before the story starts |
| Flaws that are secretly strengths | Writer sympathizes with character too much | Make at least one flaw cause real, in-story damage to someone else |
| Supporting characters who only exist for the protagonist | No ensemble pressure; the protagonist's choices feel low-stakes | Give each supporting character a Want that conflicts with others', independent of the protagonist |
| Gap moe without a core | Contradiction feels random; character seems unstable | Build the contradiction from the wound — there must be a *reason* the gap exists |
| Visual design inconsistent with character psychology | Art is crowd-tested independently; writer isn't consulted | Design brief must include Lie, Core Trait, and Gap Moe direction — art communicates these without words |
| Banner story that's just a "meet-and-greet" | No question posed, no vulnerability revealed | One specific question, one disruption, one witnessed moment of real character |
