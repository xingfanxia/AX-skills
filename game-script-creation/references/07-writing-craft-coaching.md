# 07 — Writing Craft & Adaptive Coaching

**Stream:** The prose-craft toolbox (scene, dialogue, show-don't-tell, POV, pacing, pitfalls, revision) PLUS the pedagogy layer that tells the guiding AI how to scaffold differently for different skill levels.
**Target reader:** An AI agent coaching a non-professional native-Chinese writer through actually writing scenes/dialogue. Skill level is variable and must be detected — that is the whole point of Part B.

This file carries TWO jobs. **Part A** is the craft: how to write a scene that moves. **Part B** is the coaching: how to figure out who you're talking to and adjust the help up or down so a total beginner isn't drowned in jargon and a writer-with-instinct isn't condescended to. Part B is not optional polish — it is the answer to "the skill must cover all levels fully."

> **Note on Chinese-language use.** The end writer thinks in Chinese. Craft *principles* (subtext, MRU, psychic distance) are language-agnostic, but the *examples and the coaching dialogue should be delivered in Chinese* (中文示范). # PART A — WRITING CRAFT FOR NON-PROS

## 1. Scene Construction — Swain's Scene & Sequel

### What it is
Dwight V. Swain (*Techniques of the Selling Writer*, 1965 — the foundational text most modern scene advice descends from) splits narrative into two alternating units: the **Scene** (action) and the **Sequel** (reaction). This is the single most useful structural tool for a beginner, because it turns the terrifying question "what happens next?" into a mechanical chain.

**Scene = Goal → Conflict → Disaster.**
- **Goal**: the POV character walks in *wanting* something specific and concrete, right now. ("Convince Dad to introduce himself to Bingley." Not "be happy.")
- **Conflict**: obstacles block the goal — another person, the world, the character's own limits. The scene is the *struggle*, not the wish.
- **Disaster** (a.k.a. setback): the scene does NOT resolve cleanly. Either a flat "no," a "yes, but…," or a "no, and furthermore…". The disaster is what makes the reader turn the page.

**Sequel = Reaction → Dilemma → Decision.**
- **Reaction**: the emotional fallout of the disaster (felt in the body first — see MRU below).
- **Dilemma**: the character weighs bad options. There is no obviously good move; that's what makes it a dilemma.
- **Decision**: the character commits to a new course — **and that decision becomes the Goal of the next Scene.**

### Why this chains into momentum
The decision at the end of one sequel is the goal at the start of the next scene. Disaster → reaction → decision → new goal → new disaster. That interlock is *cause and effect*, and cause-and-effect is what readers experience as "I couldn't put it down." A story that is just "and then, and then, and then" has scenes with no disasters; a story that drags in the middle usually has sequels with no dilemmas (the character just bounces to the next thing without cost).

### Before / After (中文示范)
- **Before (no scene structure):** 小林去找队长，队长同意了，他们出发去遗迹。(Three beats, zero conflict, zero disaster. Nothing is at stake; the reader feels nothing.)
- **After (scene with disaster):** 小林要队长批准夜间突袭【Goal】。队长拒绝，说人手不够，还反问他是不是想送死【Conflict】。小林亮出截获的情报，队长沉默了——然后说："准了。但你带的人，死了算你的。"【Disaster: "yes, BUT" — he got the goal at a cost that now haunts him】。
- **The sequel that follows:** 小林回到营地，手一直在抖【Reaction】。带谁去？带老兵，胜算大但都是有家室的人；带新兵，等于送死【Dilemma】。他最终敲响新兵的帐篷——因为他无法面对老兵的妻子【Decision → becomes next scene's Goal】。

### The beginner pitfall
Beginners write **scenes with no disaster** (everything the protagonist wants, they get) and **sequels with no dilemma** (feelings are stated, then the plot just moves on). The fix is a single diagnostic question per scene: *"What does the POV character want in this scene, and do they fail to get it cleanly?"* If the answer is "they get it," either add a cost or cut the scene. Note Swain's own caveat (via The Novel Smithy): don't obsess over this in a *first* draft — it's a revision lens and a planning lens, not a sentence-by-sentence straitjacket.

## 2. The Motivation-Reaction Unit (MRU)

### What it is
Also Swain. Inside a scene, prose flows as a chain of **Motivation** (something external and objective happens) → **Reaction** (the character responds). Swain's image: *"effect follows cause like a burnt finger jerking back from a hot stove."* The reaction has an internal order that mirrors real neurology:
1. **Feeling / involuntary reflex** (fastest — the flinch, the gut-drop)
2. **Action** (what they do)
3. **Speech / rational thought** (slowest)

Get them out of order and the prose feels "off" without the reader knowing why.

### Before / After
- **Before (out of order):** "I'm leaving," she said, and her stomach clenched, and she felt afraid. (Speech → feeling — backwards. The body reacts *before* the mouth.)
- **After (correct order):** Her stomach clenched. She grabbed her coat. "I'm leaving." (Feeling → action → speech. Reads as real.)

### The beginner pitfall
**Reaction before motivation** ("She ducked. The vase shattered against the wall." — she ducked before we know what she's ducking from) and **stacked reactions with no cause**. The fix: every time a character reacts, make sure the trigger appeared *one beat earlier*, and order the reaction feeling→action→speech. As with scene/sequel, flag to the writer that this is a *polishing* tool — applying it while drafting can freeze a beginner. Source: thenovelsmithy.com/the-motivation-reaction-unit.

## 3. Dialogue Craft

### 3a. Subtext — saying one thing, meaning another
**What it is:** characters rarely say what they actually want or feel. The gap between the surface line and the real meaning is **subtext** (潜台词), and it is the difference between dialogue that feels alive and dialogue that feels like a press release. "On-the-nose" dialogue (a character stating exactly what they think/feel/want, with no gap) is the cardinal beginner sin.

**Before / After (中文示范):**
- **On-the-nose:** "我很生气，因为你没打电话给我。" (States the emotion and its cause outright. Dead.)
- **Subtext:** "你的手机是用来看时间的吗？" (Surface = a question about the phone. Subtext = anger + hurt + accusation.) — pattern adapted from Randall Wallace's example: instead of *"I'm angry you didn't call,"* write *"Did you forget how to use a phone?"*

A useful test from screenwriting (Industrial Scripts, Pulp Fiction "Royale with Cheese"): when two characters talk about something *trivial* during a tense moment, the triviality itself becomes the subtext — the unspoken thing presses harder *because* no one names it.

**Pitfall:** every line says exactly what the character means. Fix: pick the most emotionally loaded line in a scene and rewrite it as something the character would say to *avoid* saying the real thing.

### 3b. The "said" rule, beats, and tags
**What it is:**
- **Dialogue tag** = "he said / she asked." **"Said" is nearly invisible** — readers' eyes skate over it. That invisibility is a feature: it attributes the line without stealing attention. (Storm Writing School.)
- **Action beat** (a.k.a. stage business) = a bit of action attached to a line instead of a tag: *She set the cup down. "I'm not going."* Beats do triple duty — they attribute the speaker, control pace, and reveal state of mind — but they **must be purposeful**, not aimless throat-clearing (Institute of Children's Literature). Aimless beats ("he nodded," "she shrugged") slow the scene and make readers hunt for meaning that isn't there.

**Stephen King (*On Writing*) on the related adverb trap:** *"The road to hell is paved with adverbs,"* and the worst place is dialogue attribution. *"'Put it down!' she shouted menacingly"* — the "menacingly" is the writer not trusting the line or the scene. If the line and the action are right, the adverb is redundant; if they're wrong, the adverb can't save them.

**Before / After:**
- **Before:** "Get out!" she screamed angrily, furiously gesturing at the door.
- **After:** She yanked the door open. "Get out." (Beat carries the fury; "said" isn't even needed; no adverb.)

**Pitfall:** "said-bookisms" (he expostulated, she ejaculated, he opined) + adverb-stuffed tags. Fix: default to "said"; replace tags with purposeful beats where you want pace or characterization; reserve non-"said" verbs (whispered, shouted) for when the *physical act of speaking* genuinely differs.

### 3c. Distinct character voice
**What it is:** if two characters' lines are interchangeable, you have one character talking to themselves. Differentiate along four axes (Career Authors; Ellen Brock): **diction** (formal vs. slang — *"Permit me a moment to assess the situation"* vs. *"Lemme take a look"*); **sentence length/structure** (ramblers vs. clipped grunts — Brock: "you always know if it's Elvis or Pike speaking, even without an attribution"); **exchange pattern** (interrupts vs. trails off (…) vs. monologues); and **what shaped them** (upbringing, education, career, worldview, generation). Restraint rule: **one accent and one verbal tic per book** — more is parody.

**Pitfall:** "everyone sounds like the author." Fixes: write a 3-line "voice swatch" per major character before drafting; edit *all of one character's lines at once* in revision to hear consistency; and run the cover-test — cover the tags; can you still tell who's speaking?

### 3d. Exposition & infodump in dialogue
**What it is:** **"As you know, Bob…"** dialogue — characters telling each other things they both already know, purely to inform the reader. It's the most transparent form of on-the-nose writing. Exposition is necessary; delivering it as a lecture is not. The principle (Industrial Scripts): *exposition is a scalpel, not a chainsaw* — feed it in the smallest doses the scene can carry, ideally *inside* conflict.

**Before / After:**
- **Before:** "As you know, Captain, our reactor has been failing for three weeks ever since the Centauri attack." (Both already know this.)
- **After:** "Three weeks." The captain didn't look up. "Tell me again how the Centauri got close enough to do this." (Same facts surface — through accusation and tension.)

**Pitfall:** characters narrating shared history at each other. Fix: ask "would these two people actually say this to each other?" If no, deliver the fact through conflict, through a newcomer who genuinely doesn't know, or through action/visual instead of speech.

## 4. Show, Don't Tell (and when to Tell)

### What it is
"Telling" reports a conclusion (*She was nervous*). "Showing" dramatizes the evidence and lets the reader *feel* it (*She kept checking her phone, then turning it face-down*). Showing uses **Concrete Sensory Detail (CSD)** — the five senses — to put the reader *inside* the moment (Linda Clare; Clay Stafford). But this is a tool, not a religion: **summary is correct** for compressing time, bridging scenes, or conveying low-stakes factual info. The skill is knowing which gear you're in.

### Filter words (滤镜词)
The most actionable sub-rule. **Filter words** are perception verbs that insert the character as a lens between reader and experience: *saw, heard, felt, noticed, watched, realized, seemed, could see/hear.* They create distance.
- **With filter:** *She heard the door creak and saw a shadow move across the floor.*
- **Without:** *The door creaked. A shadow slid across the floor.* (The reader perceives directly — closer, faster, scarier.)

### White Room Syndrome (白屋症)
Beginner scenes often happen in a featureless void — dialogue floating with no setting (Writing Mastery). The reader can't tell if two people are arguing in a kitchen or on the moon. Fix: anchor every scene in 2–3 specific sensory details, ideally ones that *do work* (reveal mood or character), e.g. a character avoiding eye contact by staring at a happy family photo on the wall.

### Before / After
- **Tell:** It was hot. (Abstract; reader feels nothing.)
- **Show:** His T-shirt clung to his back, and the cicadas droned so loud he couldn't think. (Crying in H Mart / enchantingmarketing example pattern.)

### The beginner pitfall
Two opposite failure modes: (1) **telling everything** (a synopsis pretending to be a scene), and (2) **over-correcting** — purple, every-mundane-action-dramatized prose where making coffee takes a page. Fix: dramatize the moments that carry emotional weight; summarize the connective tissue. Rule of thumb: *the more important the moment, the closer and more sensory you go.*

## 5. POV & Narrative Distance (Psychic Distance)

### What it is
**Point of view** = whose head we're in and grammatical person (1st "I", 3rd-limited "she", omniscient). **Psychic distance** (John Gardner, *The Art of Fiction*) = *how far in or out the camera sits* on a continuum, independent of person. Gardner's gift to beginners: it collapses a dozen separate decisions (POV, voice, show/tell, intimacy) into ONE question — *how far-in or far-out should we be in this sentence?* (Emma Darwin / itch of writing.)

### Gardner's five levels (the exact example progression)
1. *It was winter of the year 1853. A large man stepped out of a doorway.* — remote, objective, camera high
2. *Henry J. Warburton had never much cared for snowstorms.* — named, particular, still external
3. *Henry hated snowstorms.* — closer, personalized
4. *God how he hated these damn snowstorms.* — the character's *voice* now colors the narration
5. *Snow. Under your collar, down inside your shoes, freezing and plugging up your miserable soul…* — full interiority / stream of consciousness

### The practical rule
**Don't jump levels abruptly.** Move along the spectrum in stages — pull in from 2→3→4 as a scene heats up, ease back out 4→2 to transition or cover time. A jarring jump (from a remote establishing shot straight into stream-of-consciousness) disorients the reader (Univ. of Nevada Reno Writing Center). To change which *character* we're inside, move *out* to a far level first (where no single voice owns the narration), then back *in* to the new character — that far-out narrator is the "airlock" between two heads.

### The beginner pitfall: head-hopping
**Head-hopping** = sliding between multiple characters' interiorities *within one scene*, with no pattern, so the reader is never grounded (Alyssa Matesic; Kevin T. Johns notes new writers default to a "sort-of-third-limited / sort-of-omniscient" mush). It breaks immersion. Fix: **one POV character per scene.** Want another character's feeling? Show it externally — through what the POV character observes (a clenched jaw, a too-bright smile). True omniscience is a deliberate, consistent narrator voice, NOT random hopping, and it's hard to pull off on a first novel — steer beginners to third-limited.

## 6. Prose Rhythm, Pacing & Sentence Variety

### What it is
Ursula K. Le Guin (*Steering the Craft*): *"The chief duty of a narrative sentence is to keep the story going."* Pace and movement depend above all on **rhythm**, and you control rhythm by **hearing it — reading aloud.** Her famous demonstration of monotony vs. variety:

> *"This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous… I vary the sentence length, and I create music… And sometimes, when I am certain the reader is rested, I will engage him with a sentence of considerable length, a sentence that burns with energy and builds with all the impetus of a crescendo…"*

Short sentences = speed, tension, impact. Long flowing sentences = lyricism, reflection, immersion. **Variety is the engine.** Le Guin warns against "Macho Staccato" — the affectation of relentlessly short Hemingway-esque sentences — as its own kind of monotony; aim for *your own natural rhythm*, developed by reading good prose aloud.

### Pacing levers
Short sentences + white space + dialogue = fast (action, panic); long paragraphs + dense description + summary = slow (contemplation). King's note: the belief that commercially successful = fast-paced is "largely bullshit" — pace serves the story, and your "Ideal Reader" gauges whether backstory and pace land.

### Before / After
- **Monotone:** He opened the door. He saw the body. He felt sick. He called the police. He waited outside. (Five identical short declaratives — droning.)
- **Varied:** He opened the door. The body lay where the smell had promised — and for a long moment he simply stood there, one hand still on the knob, unable to make his legs carry him back into the hall to find a phone. (Short punch → long unspooling sentence that embodies his paralysis.)

### The beginner pitfall
Every sentence the same length and shape (usually all medium subject-verb-object). Fix: read the passage **aloud**; wherever you run out of breath or your eyes glaze, the rhythm is wrong. Deliberately drop a 3-word sentence after a long one for impact; combine three choppy sentences into one flowing one for calm.

## 7. Common Beginner Pitfalls — Catalog with Fixes

A reference table the AI can pattern-match a writer's draft against. (Sources: Helping Writers Become Authors "Most Common Writing Mistakes"; Book Butchers; Write the Magic; K.T. Johns.)

| Pitfall | What it looks like | The fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Mary Sue / overpowered protagonist** | Hero is beautiful, brilliant, universally loved, never truly fails, has no flaw that costs them | Give a real weakness that *causes a real loss*; let them fail and earn recovery. Distance from self-insert (Johns: writing a protagonist *unlike* you is freeing) |
| **Infodump** | Paragraphs/chapters of backstory or worldbuilding dropped in a block; "As you know…" dialogue | Drip it in through conflict, action, and need-to-know. King: "everyone has a history and most of it isn't very interesting — stick to the parts that are" |
| **Purple prose** | Ornate, over-adjectived, thesaurus-diving prose; "vocabulary searching for a story" | Cut adjective stacks; one strong noun/verb beats three modifiers; read aloud and delete what preens |
| **Telling not showing** | Emotions/conclusions stated (*she was sad*) | Dramatize with CSD + behavior; cut filter words (§4) |
| **Head-hopping** | POV slides between heads mid-scene | One POV per scene; show others externally (§5) |
| **Passive voice overuse** | "The ball was thrown by him"; agency hidden | Active voice by default (King rule #2: "timid writers like passive verbs"); name who does what. (Note: passive is a *tool*, fine when the actor is unknown/irrelevant — overuse is the problem) |
| **Talking-heads dialogue** | Disembodied dialogue in a white room | Anchor in setting + purposeful beats (§3b, §4) |
| **Plot armor** | Hero survives only because they're the hero; threats never feel real | Make stakes land — let secondary characters lose; ensure danger has visible cost |
| **Deus ex machina** | Problem solved by a sudden unestablished force/coincidence | Plant the solution earlier (Chekhov's gun); the hero's own choices must resolve the climax |
| **Weak stakes** | Reader doesn't know/care what happens if the hero fails | State what's lost on failure; make it personal and concrete, not abstract ("save the world") |
| **"As you know" exposition** | Characters narrate shared knowledge | §3d — conflict, newcomer, or visual |
| **Starting too early / "saggy" opening** | Pages of throat-clearing before the interesting part; opens on weather, a dream, or a mirror | Start *in* something intriguing (writingclasses.com); cut everything before the first real disturbance |
| **Flat / interchangeable characters** | Everyone sounds the same, no internal conflict | Voice swatches (§3c); Want/Need fracture (see file 02) |
| **Adverb addiction** | -ly words propping up weak verbs/tags | Stronger verb instead; delete from dialogue tags (King) |

## 8. Revision & Self-Editing

### 8a. Permission to suck — the first draft principle
**Anne Lamott, "Shitty First Drafts" (*Bird by Bird*, 1994)** — the single most important mindset for a non-pro:
> *"Almost all good writing begins with terrible first efforts. You need to start somewhere. Start by getting something — anything — down on paper."*

Her three-draft frame:
- **Down draft** — you just get it *down*. (Child's draft: "let it all pour out and romp all over the place, knowing no one will see it.")
- **Up draft** — you fix it *up*; say what you mean more accurately.
- **Dental draft** — you check every tooth, "to see if it's loose or cramped or decayed."

And the line to quote at any blocked writer: *"Perfectionism is the voice of the oppressor… It will keep you cramped and insane your whole life, and it is the main obstacle between you and a shitty first draft."*

King agrees: *"When you write a story, you're telling yourself the story. When you rewrite, your main job is taking out all the things that are NOT the story."* — i.e., **drafting and editing are different jobs; don't do them at once.** (Practical tool many coaches recommend: a timed sprint with deleting/backspacing disabled — Word focus mode, Scrivener compose — to stay in *creator* mode, not *editor* mode.)

### 8b. Layered revision — passes, not chaos
The professional secret: **revise in passes, one type of problem at a time, biggest first.** Polishing prose before fixing structure is wasted effort — you'll cut that beautiful sentence when the scene gets deleted. Synthesis of Savannah Gilbo, Lisa Poisso ("peeling the onion"), and Sol Stein:

1. **Mindset shift** — stop being the author, become the editor. Put the draft away for days/weeks first (cool-off is real).
2. **Read the whole thing without fixing anything** — just take notes.
3. **Reverse outline** — map what you *actually* wrote (not what you intended) scene by scene; this exposes structural holes.
4. **Big-picture / story pass** — plot logic, structure, character arcs, stakes, pacing. Move/cut whole scenes.
5. **Scene pass** — does each scene have a goal + disaster (§1)? Cut scenes that don't advance plot or character.
6. **Line/prose pass** — voice, rhythm, filter words, show-vs-tell, dialogue.
7. **Polish/copy pass** — grammar, typos, word repetition, the King "2nd draft = 1st draft − 10%" cut.

**Sol Stein's "Triage" (*Stein on Writing*, 1995):** don't revise front-to-back; prioritize by importance — main character, then antagonist, then conflict, then scene memorability, then minor characters. Fix what matters most first.

### 8c. Self-editing without paralysis
Over-analysis paralysis comes from comparing a rough draft to an *imagined finished book* (Killzone Blog) — they're different objects, and that comparison is "hugely unfair." Antidotes: one-problem-per-pass (never hold the whole novel in your head at once), and a hard stopping point — "edit until you can't do more," then ship it or get outside eyes. Robert Graves: *"There is no such thing as good writing, only good rewriting"* — but rewriting has to *end*.

### 8d. 交付前 10 项修订清单（结构级，配合 ref 09 的反 AI 味清单一起跑）

§8b 是修订的**流程**（分几遍、什么顺序）；这是修订的**检查项**——一份可逐条 pattern-match 的清单（综合自 McKee / Swain / Hocking 与游戏叙事实践）。交付任何一段前过一遍：**ref 09 管"AI 味 / 字句套路"，本清单管"结构 / 玩法 / 弧线"。**

1. **主题测试**：每场戏是否都在为 controlling idea（主题句）**辩护或反驳**？纯装饰、不碰主题的场景，删或改（ref 01 §1）。
2. **赌注测试**：每场戏是否以**变化**（灾难或决定）收尾？停在平衡态的场景，删（§1）。
3. **展示测试**：标出每个直接描述情绪的形容词，至少一半换成行为 / 感官（§4）。
4. **⚠️ 玩法叙事测试（ludonarrative）**：列出这场戏里玩家**实际做的动作（gameplay verb）**——它和角色宣称的价值观一致吗？不一致就是 ludonarrative dissonance：要么改玩法、要么把不和谐写成**有意的反讽**（ref 04 §3）。**这是游戏叙事独有、小说 / 影视没有的一项。**
5. **解说测试**：标出每段只为传递设定而存在的话——它在一个有目标的场景里吗？不在就戏剧化或删（§3d、ref 03）。
6. **声音测试**：盖住说话人标签，还能认出谁在说吗（§3c）？
7. **角色弧测试**：主角的"谎言"在这一稿里被削弱或强化了吗？两者都没有 = 弧线死了（ref 02）。
8. **节奏测试**："全剧最低点 / All Is Lost"落在大致正确的位置吗（救猫咪 ~75%）？不对就重构第二幕（ref 01 §2.4 / §3.4）。
9. **世界观蔓延测试**：有没有冒出某个**不被任何节拍需要**的新设定？删，除非它挣到了位置（ref 03 §5）。
10. **（二游）伏笔节奏 + 演出测试**：打开的循环有没有计划好的回收版本（ref 10 谜）？关键情绪点有没有演出配合、还是静态对话墙（ref 05 §7.3、ref 11 §7）？

> 不是每段都要全过 10 项——但 **主题 / 赌注 / 玩法叙事** 这三项是任何场景都该过的最小集。

# PART B — ADAPTIVE COACHING / PEDAGOGY

This is where the skill earns "adapts to the user's real experience level." Each item: **principle → how the guiding AI applies it live → per-level adjustment.**

## 9. Plotter vs. Pantser (outliner vs. discovery writer)

### Principle
Writers fall on a **spectrum** from **plotter** (architect — needs a detailed outline before drafting, gets blocked *without* a roadmap) to **pantser** (discovery writer / "gardener" — starts with a vague idea and finds the story *by writing it*, gets blocked *with* a rigid outline). Most people are **plantsers** (hybrid) — and a useful sub-type is the **"headlights writer"** who can only see as far ahead as their headlights reach: plans a chapter or two, writes them, then plans the next stretch (editsbytoni; The Write Practice; IngramSpark). Pantsers typically frontload less and rewrite more; plotters frontload the outlining work. **Neither is correct** — forcing the wrong one *causes* blockage.

### How the AI applies it live
Detect the writer's type *early* with diagnostic questions, then offer the matching process. Detection questions:
- "When you imagine writing, are you more excited to *plan the whole thing out* or to *just start and see what happens*?"
- "Have you ever made a detailed outline? Did it help you write — or did it kill your interest because the story already felt 'done'?"
- "Do you usually know the ending before you start, or do you discover it?"

Then adapt the *whole skill flow*: a **plotter** wants the worldview→framework→theme→character scaffolding fully built before writing scenes (this skill's default funnel suits them). A **pantser** will rebel against heavy upfront structure — for them, get to *writing an actual scene fast*, then reverse-engineer the worldview/theme from what emerged. Offer plantsers a "headlights" rhythm: lock the next 1–2 beats, write, re-plan.

### Per-level adjustment
- **Blank slate:** don't even use the words "plotter/pantser." Just try one short scene with a tiny prompt; observe whether they freeze without structure or freeze *with* it, and infer their type from behavior.
- **Has-ideas-no-craft:** name the spectrum, let them self-identify, pick a process. This often unblocks them ("oh, I'm not *bad at outlining*, I'm a discovery writer").
- **Has-instinct-some-practice:** they likely already know their type — ask, then get out of the way; offer hybrid tools (beat sheets, reverse outlines) as options, not mandates.
- **Competent:** treat type as known; collaborate on process, don't teach it.

## 10. The Role of Constraints in Creativity

### Principle
A blank page with infinite freedom is **paralyzing**; constraints unblock (To Write With Wild Abandon; Brevity Blog; Udemy). Mechanism: limits convert "fill this void" (impossible) into "solve this challenge" (doable), and narrowing the choice-set from many to few lets the writer *commit*. Amy Goldmacher's testimony: she couldn't write with infinite time and a blank doc, but produced a finished spy story given a forced genre + location + object + 48-hour deadline. **"Writing freedom often isn't."** Three constraint types that reliably work: **time** limits, **word-count** limits, **theme/element** prompts. Constraints also force writers out of their tropes and comfort zone (unlike prompts, which give a path; constraints force experimentation).

### How the AI applies it live
**Never hand a non-pro a blank page.** When asking them to write *anything*, attach constraints: "Write the moment she realizes he lied — in under 150 words, set entirely in one room, no dialogue tags except 'said'." The constraint *is* the scaffold. For idea-generation, give 3 concrete options rather than "what do you want to write?" Use **zero-stakes warm-ups** unrelated to their project to bypass the cursor-fear ("write a paragraph about two strangers stuck in an elevator" — note the Chinese-language ChatGPT-作家指南 source recommends exactly this).

### Per-level adjustment
- **Blank slate:** maximum constraint — tiny word count, single setting, supply the opening line, even supply the first sentence and ask them to continue. Constraints carry almost all the cognitive load.
- **Has-ideas-no-craft:** medium constraint — give a scene goal + a limit ("get character A to refuse character B, but A can't say the word 'no'"). Enough freedom to express their idea, enough rail to prevent flailing.
- **Has-instinct-some-practice:** light constraint — a single challenge to stretch them past a known weakness ("you over-explain emotions — write this grief scene with no emotion words at all").
- **Competent:** offer constraints as optional creative provocations, not training wheels.

## 11. How Creative Writing Is Actually Taught — Workshop, Feedback, Scaffolding, Socratic

### 11a. The workshop method (and its trap)
**Principle:** the dominant pedagogy (the "Iowa model") puts a draft in front of readers who critique it. But it is **widely criticized** for crushing emerging/marginalized writers (Junot Díaz's "MFA vs. POC"; Zoë Bossiere; Felicia Rose Chavez) — at its worst it discourages promising writers into quitting. Modern correctives (Gabrielle Fuentes via Liz Lerman's *Critical Response Process*; bell hooks) are **dialogue-based and writer-led**: readers first say what's *meaningful/working*, the *author asks the questions*, and critique doesn't have to be the closing note.

**How the AI applies it live:** you are a one-on-one workshop, so adopt the *corrected* model, never the gladiator-pit version. Lead with what works and is meaningful, let the *writer* drive what they want help on, and frame critique as a reader's experience ("here's where I as a reader got confused"), not a verdict.

### 11b. Feedback: sandwich vs. direct — and the better third option
**Principle:** the **feedback sandwich** (praise–criticism–praise) is now under scrutiny (SessionLab): it can feel manipulative and muddy the actual ask. The better structures are action-oriented:
- **Start / Stop / Continue** — "Start doing X, stop doing Y, keep doing Z." Concrete and forward-looking.
- **Feedback wrap** — give *context + observation + impact + suggestion* so it's not a blame game.

**How the AI applies it live:** prefer Start/Stop/Continue over the sandwich. Always pair every criticism with a *specific next action* ("this scene has no disaster → add a cost to what she wants"). Genuine, specific praise (not "great job!") is load-bearing for a fragile beginner — but it must be *true*, or it teaches nothing.

### 11c. Scaffolding — Gradual Release of Responsibility ("I do / we do / you do")
**Principle:** the canonical scaffold (Pearson & Gallagher 1983; NSW Dept of Education) moves cognitive load from teacher to learner in phases:
1. **I do** — model it. Demonstrate with a worked example + think-aloud.
2. **We do** — guided practice. Co-create; the AI and writer write together, AI supporting in real time.
3. **You do (together)** — collaborative; writer leads, AI is a safety net.
4. **You do (alone)** — independent; writer produces, AI reviews after.

Crucially, support is released **only as fast as evidence allows** — you check for understanding before fading.

**How the AI applies it live:** this is the master move for "write-FOR vs. write-WITH vs. prompt-the-user-to-write." Pick the phase based on level (see §13). E.g. teaching subtext: **I do** = AI rewrites an on-the-nose line into subtext and explains the move; **we do** = AI and writer rewrite the next line together; **you do** = writer rewrites the third line, AI critiques.

### 11d. Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) and fading
**Principle:** the **ZPD** is the gap between what a learner can do *alone* and *with help*. Effective teaching targets that zone — tasks slightly beyond independent reach, with a safety net. **Scaffolding is contingent** (high support when new, withdrawn as competence grows); the goal is **"fading"** until the writer is self-regulating (Wood/Bruner/Ross). Over-supporting a capable writer stalls growth; under-supporting a beginner produces discouragement.

**How the AI applies it live:** continuously calibrate difficulty to *just past* current ability, and actively *reduce* help as the writer improves within a session. Nail a subtext rewrite → next ask is harder/less-supported, not the same.

### 11e. Socratic / non-directive vs. directive — the writing-center debate
**Principle:** the core tension in tutoring (Highline College Writing Center; Jeff Brooks' "minimalist tutoring"; Shamoon & Burns' critique):
- **Non-directive / minimalist (Socratic):** the tutor *asks questions* so the writer finds their own answers, keeps the pen, retains ownership ("we work with the writer, not the writing"). Builds independent writers and intrinsic motivation. Brooks: *"the less we do to a paper, the better… the object is not the paper, but the student."*
- **Directive:** the tutor *shows, models, sometimes tells* — Shamoon & Burns argue writers learn enormously by **watching and imitating an expert**, and that pure non-directivity can be a withholding pose that fails writers who genuinely need to be shown how.

The resolution is a **continuum**, not a binary (Corbett) — meet the individual at *their* level. Address **Higher-Order Concerns first** (HOCs: structure, content, argument, character, stakes) and **Lower-Order Concerns second** (LOCs: grammar, mechanics, word choice). Fixing commas on a scene you'll later cut is backwards.

**How the AI applies it live:** **default to Socratic for ideas and ownership** ("what does your character actually want here?" beats "make her want revenge") — this draws out *their* story instead of writing yours over it. **Switch to directive/modeling for craft mechanics** a beginner has never seen — you cannot Socratically extract the concept of "filter words" from someone who's never heard of it; show, then have them imitate. And always triage HOCs before LOCs.

## 12. Novice vs. Expert Writers — what's actually different (Bereiter & Scardamalia)

### Principle
The key cognitive research (Bereiter & Scardamalia, *The Psychology of Written Composition*, 1987) names two modes:
- **Knowledge-telling (novice):** the writer probes memory with a topic/genre cue and writes down whatever comes up, in roughly the order it surfaces. Content retrieval = text generation; there's **no goal-setting, no sub-goals, no shaping for a reader.** It "gets the job done" and is efficient — but it *reproduces* what the writer already knows.
- **Knowledge-transforming (expert):** the writer sets rhetorical goals, and the act of writing becomes a *problem-solving dialogue* between what they want to say and how to say it — they **discover and refine** what they think *through* writing. Writing *changes* their knowledge, doesn't just dump it.

This is *why* novice and expert writing differ, and it dictates the coaching: a novice isn't a defective expert — they're running a *different process*. You can't just say "set rhetorical goals"; you scaffold the transformation.

### How the AI applies it live
Recognize knowledge-telling in a draft (info arrives in memory-order, not reader-order; everything the writer knows about a character is stated at once; no apparent awareness of what the reader needs *when*). Don't shame it — it's developmentally normal. Move them toward transforming by inserting the missing reader-goal: "What do you want the *reader* to feel/believe by the end of this scene? Now — does every line serve that?" That single reframe (write for the reader's experience, not to empty your memory) is the novice→expert bridge.

## 13. THE SKILL-LEVEL CALIBRATION RUBRIC (key deliverable)

Four levels. The AI **detects** via signals, then **adjusts** the dials: hand-holding, jargon, write-FOR/WITH/prompt ratio, step length, theory dose, GRR phase, directive/Socratic balance, constraint strength.

> **Detection method (do this lightly, conversationally, in the first 1–3 exchanges — never administer it as a test):** ask the three questions from §9 (plotter/pantser), ask "have you written stories before, even for fun / as a kid / fanfic?", and — most reliable — **ask for one tiny sample** ("write me 3 sentences where someone gets bad news"). The sample reveals level faster than any self-report, because beginners *over-rate* (Dunning-Kruger) or *under-rate* (impostor syndrome) themselves. Re-calibrate continuously; a writer can be Level 3 on instinct but Level 1 on dialogue specifically.

### Level 0 — Blank Slate (never written fiction; possibly intimidated)
- **Signals:** "I have no idea how to start," asks permission a lot, freezes at open questions, the sample is summary not scene, or they can't produce a sample at all. May express fear ("I'm not a writer").
- **Hand-holding:** maximum. Tiny steps, one concept at a time, lots of genuine encouragement. Lead with Lamott's "permission to suck" (§8a) *before* any craft.
- **Jargon:** near-zero. Say "what they say vs. what they really mean" before ever uttering "subtext." Introduce one Chinese term at a time, only after they've *felt* the concept in an example.
- **Write FOR/WITH/prompt:** mostly **write-FOR + I-do modeling.** AI produces a model scene, names what it did simply, then "you change one thing." Heavy co-writing (we-do).
- **Step length:** micro. 50–150 word asks. One scene beat, not a chapter.
- **Theory dose:** almost none surfaced. Theory lives behind the curtain; the writer just *does* small successful things and builds confidence.
- **Constraints:** maximum (§10) — supply opening line, single setting, tight word cap.
- **Directive/Socratic:** mostly **directive/modeling** for craft; gentle Socratic for *their* ideas so the story stays theirs. **HOCs only** — never mention grammar.
- **Primary goal:** *get a win on the page and kill the fear.* Quantity of finished tiny things > quality.

### Level 1 — Has Ideas, No Craft (rich imagination, can't execute)
- **Signals:** vivid concepts/characters/world in their head, but the prose tells-not-shows, dialogue is on-the-nose, info-dumps, head-hops. The sample has *energy* but breaks craft rules. Often very motivated.
- **Hand-holding:** high but decreasing. They don't need motivation; they need technique.
- **Jargon:** introduce craft terms *as they hit the relevant problem* — teach "show don't tell" when their draft tells, "filter words" when you spot them. Just-in-time, problem-triggered.
- **Write FOR/WITH/prompt:** **we-do dominant.** Co-write to demonstrate a technique on *their* material, then hand it back. Before/after rewrites of *their own lines* are gold here.
- **Step length:** small-to-medium. A full short scene with a goal+disaster.
- **Theory dose:** moderate, *applied*. One craft principle per session, immediately practiced on their work — never abstract lecture.
- **Constraints:** medium (§10) — scene goal + one limitation targeting a specific weakness.
- **Directive/Socratic:** balanced. **Directive for the craft mechanic** (show the fix), **Socratic for story choices** (draw out their intent). Introduce knowledge-transforming (§12): "what should the reader feel by line's end?"
- **Primary goal:** install core craft moves (scene structure, show-don't-tell, subtext, one POV) on material they already care about.

### Level 2 — Has Instinct, Some Practice (writes okay by feel, no formal training)
- **Signals:** the sample mostly *works* — scenes have some tension, dialogue has some subtext — but inconsistently, and they can't *name why* a passage works or fails. They sense problems ("this part drags") without a vocabulary or a fix.
- **Hand-holding:** low. Respect their instinct; don't re-teach basics they've absorbed intuitively (condescension is the failure mode here).
- **Jargon:** full craft vocabulary now *useful* — it gives names to things they already do by feel, which makes the skill *repeatable* (this is the knowledge-telling→transforming leap). "What you did there is called psychic distance — and here's how to control it on purpose."
- **Write FOR/WITH/prompt:** **prompt-the-writer dominant** + you-do. They write; AI critiques with specifics and the occasional targeted model for a technique they haven't met.
- **Step length:** medium-to-large. Full scenes, scene sequences, revision passes.
- **Theory dose:** higher and *systematizing* — connect the tools (Swain + psychic distance + pacing) into a coherent toolbox so their instinct becomes a method.
- **Constraints:** light (§10) — stretch-challenges aimed at named weaknesses.
- **Directive/Socratic:** **Socratic dominant.** Ask questions that make them diagnose their own draft ("read this aloud — where does the rhythm flatten?"). Begin **LOC work** alongside HOCs.
- **Primary goal:** convert instinct into conscious, repeatable craft + introduce layered revision (§8b).

### Level 3 — Competent (drafts complete, mostly clean; wants to level up)
- **Signals:** the sample is solid — consistent POV, working dialogue, varied rhythm. Problems are subtle (theme coherence, structural sag, voice distinction across a cast). Talks craft fluently.
- **Hand-holding:** minimal. Peer-collaborator stance.
- **Jargon:** full, including advanced (Stein's triage, Le Guin's rhythm analysis, knowledge-transforming as an explicit aim).
- **Write FOR/WITH/prompt:** **you-do-alone.** They produce; AI is editor/reviewer/sounding-board. Rarely models — only for genuinely novel advanced techniques.
- **Step length:** large — whole scenes, arcs, manuscripts; revision strategy over line edits.
- **Theory dose:** as much as they want; engage as equals, debate craft choices.
- **Constraints:** optional creative provocations only.
- **Directive/Socratic:** **almost fully Socratic + reader-feedback.** "Here's where I as a reader disengaged" + targeted questions. Full HOC→LOC pass.
- **Primary goal:** refine, deepen theme/coherence, polish voice, manage revision at scale.

### Cross-cutting calibration rules
1. **Detect by sample, not self-report** — and re-detect per skill (level on dialogue ≠ level on structure).
2. **Fail gracefully upward:** if unsure between two levels, start *one level lower* in support but with the *higher* level's respect — easier to fade help than to rescue a discouraged writer.
3. **Fade within the session:** when a writer nails something, immediately reduce support / raise difficulty (ZPD, §11d). Don't keep them on training wheels they've outgrown.
4. **Match the funnel to type** (§9): plotters tolerate this skill's full worldview→theme→character build-up; pantsers need to write a scene *first*, then back-fill — don't force the funnel on a discovery writer or you'll block them.
5. **Impostor syndrome is near-universal** at every level (a study found 90%+ of *experienced* writers feel it) — normalize it, separate fear-disguised-as-logic from real assessment, and remember Lamott: *"If I write, I am a writer. That's the only qualification."*

## Uncertainty flags
- The Gardner five-level example sentences (§5) are reproduced via Emma Darwin's widely-cited gloss of *The Art of Fiction*, not Gardner's original page — wording of levels 4–5 may differ slightly from the source text. The five-level structure and the no-abrupt-jumps rule are well-corroborated across multiple writing-center sources.
- Bereiter & Scardamalia knowledge-telling/transforming (§12) is drawn from secondary summaries and ERIC PDFs of the 1987 work, which is consistent across sources; exact phrasings are paraphrased.
- Swain's scene/sequel and MRU (§1–2) are from secondary expositions (Weiland, The Novel Smithy) of *Techniques of the Selling Writer*; the structure is universally consistent but Swain's exact terminology ("disaster" vs. "setback") varies by source.
- "Feedback sandwich is now discouraged" (§11b) reflects current L&D/pedagogy opinion (SessionLab and others), not unanimous consensus — some practitioners still defend a well-executed sandwich.
- Workshop-model critique (§11a) is contested terrain; the "Iowa model harms marginalized writers" position (Díaz, Chavez) is influential but represents one (strong) scholarly camp.
