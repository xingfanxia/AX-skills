# 03 — Worldbuilding Methodology
## Reference for `game-script-creation` skill · Stream: World (世界观)

> **Reader note:** This file is used by an AI agent guiding a non-professional, native Chinese-speaking writer. All guidance should translate into concrete next actions, not abstract theory. Examples skew toward 二次元手游 (anime gacha mobile games) where genre is relevant.

---

## 1. Systematic Worldbuilding Dimensions

### What it is
A coherent world rests on eight interlocking dimensions. These aren't sequential tasks — they're a *tension network*. Change one and the others shift.

| Dimension | Core question | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Geography / Environment 地理** | Where do people live, and what does the land force them to do? | Constrains trade routes, siege warfare, disease spread, where cities can exist |
| **History / Timeline 历史** | What was destroyed, and why does it haunt the present? | Ruins, grudges, forbidden knowledge, cultural memory |
| **Factions / Powers 势力** | Who wants what, and who stands in their way? | Conflict engine; readers track faction logic, not abstract history |
| **Culture / Peoples 文化** | What do people celebrate, fear, and take for granted? | Makes characters feel native to a world, not tourists |
| **Religion / Belief 信仰** | What do people think is sacred or forbidden, and are they right? | Moral stakes; explains behavior that looks irrational by secular logic |
| **Politics / Economy 政治经济** | Who decides, and who pays? | Power balance; magic access, resource wars, class tension |
| **Tech / Magic System 技术/力量体系** | What can people do that they couldn't do in the real world? | Defines the problem-solving toolkit; must be consistent |
| **Daily Life 日常** | What does a normal person eat, fear, joke about today? | Ground-level texture; prevents a world that only exists at plot-scale |

### Minimum Viable Worldview (MVW) — the canvas

Before you write Scene 1, you need answers to exactly this much:

```
世界观画布 (Worldview Canvas)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
核心前提 (Core Conceit):   [One sentence — what's the ONE impossible thing?]
地理一句话 (Geography):     [Climate + landmark that affects the story]
当前紧张态势 (Tension):     [Which faction wants what and who opposes them?]
力量规则 (Power Rule):      [What can magic/tech do, and what is its cost?]
主角所在阶层 (Character's stratum): [Where in this society does the protagonist stand?]
文化一条禁忌 (Taboo):       [One thing people here simply do not do]
```

That's six fields. Fill them before the first draft. Everything else you build *while* writing, triggered by story need.

### Concrete example
**明日方舟 (Arknights):** The world's core geographic pressure is "天灾" (catastrophic wandering disasters) that make permanent cities impossible — everything travels on "方舟" mobile fortresses. This single geographic rule generates the political structure (nomadic city-states), the economy (resource scarcity + monopolies over rare materials), the class system (infected/non-infected apartheid), and the faction conflicts. The full world map wasn't even released until 18 months after launch — players inferred it from fragments.

### Beginner pitfall
Building all eight dimensions equally before writing anything. You'll have 50,000 words of lore and zero story. **The fix:** identify which 2–3 dimensions your plot *directly* touches, build those first, stub the rest.

## 2. Magic / Power System Design — Sanderson's Three Laws

### What it is
Brandon Sanderson's published laws (essays at brandonsanderson.com, 2007–2013) are the most cited framework for designing power systems. They apply equally to sci-fi "technology systems," cultivation levels (修炼体系), or any rule-bound ability.

### The Three Laws — precise formulation

**First Law:** *"An author's ability to solve conflict with magic is DIRECTLY PROPORTIONAL to how well the reader understands said magic."*
- The more you explain a system, the more you're allowed to use it to solve story problems.
- Unknown magic = atmosphere only. Known magic = plot tool.
- Corollary: if you want a climactic magical solution, you must establish the rules *before* the problem.

**Second Law:** *"Limitations are more interesting than powers."*
- The constraint creates drama. Unlimited power = no tension.
- Costs can be: physical (blood, lifespan), material (rare components), relational (someone else pays), social (user becomes feared/hunted), cognitive (sanity), or narrative (villain also gains access).
- Sanderson's Allomancy example: every metal has an *exact* effect, every use consumes the metal, swallowing the wrong metal poisons you. The system generates betrayal plots, economic plots, and personal sacrifice plots *because of its limits*.

**Third Law:** *"Expand what you have before adding something new."*
- Explore the implications of existing rules before adding new abilities.
- "New power for new problem" = lazy writing. "Existing power used cleverly for unexpected problem" = satisfying.
- Ask: what are the *social, economic, political consequences* of this power existing? Mine those before adding powers 2 and 3.

### Hard vs Soft Magic

| Type | Definition | When to use | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hard magic (硬魔法)** | Rules, limits, costs are explicit. Reader understands the system. | When magic will *solve* plot problems. Allows reader to feel clever alongside hero. | Sanderson's Allomancy (Mistborn), 崩坏3战斗体系 |
| **Soft magic (软魔法)** | Mysterious, undefined, unknowable. Creates wonder and dread. | When magic is *atmosphere*, not problem-solver. Sense of scale, awe, or horror. | Tolkien's Gandalf, 明日方舟的"源石技艺"模糊边界 |

**Critical rule:** Don't mix them carelessly. A soft magic system that suddenly solves a hard plot problem feels like a cheat. A hard magic system with mysterious exceptions undermines player trust.

### For gacha games specifically
Players theorize and optimize — they *will* find every inconsistency in your system. Hard magic is safer for skill-kit design. Soft magic works for lore backstory (e.g., the true nature of Originium in Arknights, the Imaginary Tree in Honkai: Star Rail) because it's never the *solution* to a battle, only the *context*.

### Beginner pitfall
Making magic so expensive that the hero never uses it, or so free that it solves every problem. **The fix:** write your magic system's *stress test* — three scenarios where a character tries to misuse it. If nothing breaks, add a cost.

## 3. The Iceberg Principle — Showing Depth Without Telling It

### What it is
Hemingway: *"There is seven-eighths of an iceberg underwater for every part that shows."* Applied to worldbuilding: you must know 10x more than you reveal. The 90% underground makes the 10% feel solid. But you *reveal* only what earns its place in a scene.

### The Hemingway / Sanderson divide
- **Hemingway's position:** You actually need to know all the underwater material — readers sense the gaps when you fake it.
- **Sanderson's counter:** You can *imply* a rich world by getting specific, tactile details right and letting readers extrapolate. You don't need a complete history of the blacksmith guild; you need the blacksmith to use the right vocabulary and reference one specific past event.

For non-professional writers, the **Sanderson approach is safer** — it prevents the trap of never finishing because the lore isn't complete.

### Techniques for implying depth cheaply

1. **Incidental specificity:** A character says "since the Collapse of Year 7" — not explained. One detail implies a history.
2. **NPC casual reference:** "Even the road-wardens know not to enter the Veldt after equinox." The reader knows something; you don't explain it.
3. **Visible consequences without causes:** Ancient ruins with no exposition. Players/readers ask "what happened here?" — that question is the engagement.
4. **Character assumptions:** Characters don't explain their own culture any more than a real person explains why they remove their shoes at the door. They just do it, and the reader infers.
5. **In-world documents:** Item descriptions, found letters, graffiti. The reader does archaeological work. Extremely powerful in games.

### Environmental storytelling (game-specific)

In games, the environment IS the narration. The reader/player moves through it physically. Dark Souls (FromSoftware) makes this canonical:
- **Item placement:** A boss's armor set is placed near their corpse, explaining their defeat without a cutscene.
- **Architecture as history:** Anor Londo's scale tells you it was built for giants; the scale mismatch with current inhabitants implies a fallen civilization.
- **Enemy behavior:** A hollow soldier still patrolling a post he was ordered to hold centuries ago — behavioral storytelling.
- **Zero infodump:** Players who want the story read item descriptions; players who don't can ignore them entirely. The world serves *both* audiences.

### For 二游
Gacha games fragment lore across **multiple delivery channels**:
- 干员档案 (character profiles, 4–8 entries per character)
- 语音台词 (voice lines triggered by player interaction)
- 活动剧情 (event stories, often the deepest lore per character)
- 图鉴/档案室 (in-game encyclopedias/archives)
- 书页/信件/内部文件 (in-world documents, often optional)
- 外传漫画/音频剧/设定集 (media outside the game itself)

Arknights' world map wasn't released for 18 months — yet players built extensive wiki entries from scattered Operator file fragments. The 90% was delivered *after* the 10% created demand for it. This is intentional.

**Key design insight for 二游:** Design your world to have *three tiers of depth*:
- Tier 1 (必须知道 — must-know): main story, visible world rules, faction alignment
- Tier 2 (好奇者得): character files, optional lore books — rewards the curious
- Tier 3 (狂热者专属): cross-patch callbacks, hidden document chains, lore that requires external reading to decode

### Beginner pitfall
**Lore walls (信息倾泻):** A character stops the scene to explain how their world's history works for three paragraphs. This always reads as the author speaking, not the world breathing. **The fix:** cut the explanation. Put the conclusion of that history in a *visible consequence* instead — a scar, a law, an empty city, a forbidden name.

## 4. Internal Consistency — The "One Big Lie" Principle

### What it is
Every speculative world has one (or a small number of) impossible premises — the core conceit. **The "One Big Lie" rule:** pick your impossible premise, then derive all consequences *honestly* from it. Stop adding new lies to explain inconsistencies; that compounds the problem.

Source: Jay Angeline's formulation of The Expanse's approach. Ty Franck and Daniel Abraham picked one lie ("We can travel the solar system at constant 1G acceleration") and followed it with extraordinary rigor: the political structure of the Belt, the health consequences for belters (taller, thinner, bones that couldn't survive Earth gravity), the economics of water and oxygen in space, the time-distortion of interplanetary communication — all derived from the single premise.

### The derivation chain method
```
起点 (Core Lie): [Your impossible thing]
    ↓
物理/物质后果 (Physical/material consequence): [What does physics now allow/forbid?]
    ↓
经济后果 (Economic consequence): [Who controls this, who profits, who suffers?]
    ↓
社会/阶级后果 (Social/class consequence): [How does it stratify people?]
    ↓
政治后果 (Political consequence): [Who holds power because of it?]
    ↓
宗教/信仰后果 (Religious consequence): [What do people believe about it?]
    ↓
文化/日常后果 (Cultural/daily-life consequence): [How does it change small habits?]
```

**Example — apply to 明日方舟:**
- Core Lie: Landship disasters (天灾) make fixed cities impossible
- Physical: All cities are mobile; terrain determines political power (who can travel where)
- Economic: Resources are scarce; the infected are a disposable labor pool
- Social: Class = physical mobility; the infected are quarantined because they're expensive
- Political: Rhodes Island is simultaneously pharmacy, military, and neutral state — only possible because no one owns land
- Religious: The Sarkaz carry a theological weight (demon-race stigma) that pre-dates the game's current era
- Cultural: People in Lungmen bribe officials; people on Columbia trust legal documents more — two different survival strategies from two different mobile-city histories

This derivation chain can be run *backward* too: if you find a plot element you want (say, a black market for infected labor), trace it up the chain to make sure your core lie *actually generates* that element.

### Tolkien's framing — Secondary Belief
Tolkien's concept of "Secondary World" (from *On Fairy-Stories*, 1947): inside a well-constructed fictional world, what the creator relates is *true* within that world's laws. The reader enters "Secondary Belief" — willing suspension of disbelief maintained by internal consistency. Break the internal rules once, and Secondary Belief collapses. The reader re-enters Primary World skepticism and never fully returns.

This is why players rage when a power system is violated for plot convenience: the violation *is* the betrayal.

### Beginner pitfall
**Inconsistency stacking:** Each new plot problem gets a new hand-wave ("magic can *also* do this"). Every addition weakens the foundation. **The fix:** when you face a plot problem that your existing system can't solve, that's a story problem — solve it with character, not new rules. If you genuinely need an expansion, run the derivation chain: what are the *consequences* of this new ability? If you can't answer that without breaking other established facts, it doesn't belong.

## 5. The Worldbuilding-as-Procrastination Trap

### What it is
"Worldbuilder's Disease" — building endless lore while never writing the story. It's sneaky because it *feels* productive. It is the #1 failure mode for non-professional writers with rich imaginations.

Diagnostic signs:
- Your background notes exceed the word count of your actual draft
- You've named every district of the capital city but don't know what happens in Chapter 3
- You feel anxious starting the story because "the world isn't finished yet"
- You keep adding factions, species, or history "to understand things better"

The world is *never* finished. Professional authors write with incomplete worlds all the time.

### The correct relationship between world and story
**World serves story. Story does not serve world.**

Sanderson's operational principle: identify 2–4 worldbuilding elements to *actively explore* in your story, and leave the rest as subtext and implication. The reader doesn't need your complete cosmology; they need enough to care about this character in this crisis.

**Test every worldbuilding session with this question:** "Does this fact I just created *directly* affect a character decision, create a plot obstacle, or explain a visible consequence in my current story?" If the answer is no — stop and write the story instead.

### The "one street" principle (George Lucas rule)
You don't need to build all of Mos Eisley — you need to build the one street your characters walk down, *in detail*. A few high-resolution specifics signal an entire world. General descriptions signal an incomplete one.

Applied to game writing: design the *corner* of the world the next patch covers. Design it with dense, tactile specificity. Imply the rest.

### Minimum viable world for different formats

| Format | Minimum before writing |
|---|---|
| Short story / single scene | Core conceit + protagonist's immediate environment + one rule that constrains them |
| Novel chapter / game episode | Full MVW canvas (6 fields above) + 2 faction details + power system cost |
| Full game worldbuild | MVW canvas + 3-tier lore depth plan + 5 derivation chain consequences |
| Gacha game (cross-patch) | MVW canvas + Tier 1 lore fully written + Tier 2/3 delivery channels defined, *content can be blank* |

### For non-professional writers specifically
You have been told worldbuilding is important. It is. But the story *teaches you the world* as you write it. Characters make decisions that reveal what your world must be. Trust the process: **write → discover → worldbuild what the story needs → write more.**

**Anti-pattern to resist:** The elaborate setting document that becomes a novel no one reads because the actual story never got written.

## 6. Worldbuilding for Games — Environmental Storytelling + Gacha Delivery

### How games are fundamentally different from novels
In a novel, the author controls reading sequence. In a game, the player controls traversal. This changes everything:

- **The reader becomes an archaeologist,** not a passive recipient
- **Sequence is unpredictable** — you can't guarantee player encounters events in narrative order
- **Passive and active players coexist** — casual players want plot clarity; hardcore players want hidden depth. Both must be satisfied simultaneously.

### Environmental storytelling toolkit for game writers

| Tool | How it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Architecture / ruins (建筑/废墟)** | Physical scale and decay imply historical scale | Dark Souls: the massive architecture of Anor Londo implies it was built for beings larger than humans |
| **Item descriptions (道具描述)** | 2–4 sentences per item can deliver entire sub-plots | Dark Souls: "Used to be a carving tool, now worn smooth by the hands of countless hollows searching for their purpose" |
| **Enemy / NPC behavior (行为)** | What an NPC does, where they stand, what they say when unprompted | A merchant who flinches at the name of a city tells you something happened there without a scene |
| **Visual contrast (视觉对比)** | A wealthy district adjacent to a slum communicates more than description | 原神's Liyue: the Harbor versus the Qingce Village layout implies economic stratification |
| **Negative space (空缺)** | What's *missing* from a scene | An empty throne; a child's bedroom preserved in a deserted house; an altar with its idol removed |
| **In-world documents (内部文件)** | Letters, diaries, official records found in environment | Arknights operator files: classified permission tiers 4–8 imply secrets the player isn't cleared for |

### Gacha-specific lore architecture (二游碎片化叙事)

**Core design principle:** Gacha games deliver lore across patches over *years*. The world must be designed so each fragment satisfies *and* creates demand for the next.

**先建阵营，再建角色（gacha 世界观的生成顺序）：** 一个角色属于一个**阵营**，一个阵营属于一个**地区**，一个地区属于一个**母题/主题**。先把这条"主题 → 地区 → 阵营"的骨架搭好，再往里填角色，能让你批量产出几十个**既彼此区分又整体自洽**的角色（原神元素七国、明日方舟罗德岛/整合运动/萨卡兹/维多利亚、蔚蓝档案各学院都是这套）。反过来"先有一个酷角色再硬凑他属于哪"，到第 20 个角色时世界就散了。这也呼应 §1.2 米哈游"世界观/主题 → 角色 → 剧情"的生产顺序。**世界对 gacha 而言是一套"角色许可系统"：每个地区/阵营都在回答"为什么这种视觉/能力/性格的角色会存在于此"。**

**Delivery channel map:**

```
Tier 1 — 主线强制接触 (All players see this)
  主线剧情 → core world premise, major faction conflict, protagonist's stakes

Tier 2 — 主动探索 (Curious players discover this)
  干员档案 (Operator files) → character backstory, world geography details
  活动剧情 (Event stories) → deep faction lore, character development
  图鉴/档案室 (Codex) → world ecology, history, politics

Tier 3 — 狂热者专属 (Fans seek this out)
  隐藏书页/信件 (Hidden documents) → cross-references require prior Tier 2 knowledge
  外部媒体 (Manga, audio dramas, artbooks) → canonical but not in-game
  跨版本伏笔 (Cross-patch callbacks) → details from 2 years ago suddenly matter
```

**Arknights as case study:**
- The Terran world map was not released until 18 months post-launch. Players built accurate fan maps from Operator file geographic references before the official map existed.
- "Classified Permission Tier 8" entries in Operator files imply secrets so significant they're withheld even in the archive — a brilliant negative-space technique.
- The Lungmen arc (a single city) required 3+ major story chapters across 2 years, each revealing a new layer. No infodump; pure sequenced revelation.

**Genshin Impact's geographic lore delivery:**
- Each new nation independently reconstructs the "god-civilization" relationship through environmental archaeology (landmarks, weapon descriptions, ancient murals).
- Player community inference is treated as a *feature*, not a gap — ambiguity is maintained intentionally because fan analysis extends the game's cultural life between patches.
- Formula: `Core plot exposure = 0.3 × direct narration + 0.5 × environmental storytelling + 0.2 × player community inference` (from game studies analysis)

### The patch-reveal design loop for 二游

```
新版本 (New patch)
  → 主线章节 reveals: [One major world fact, one major character revelation, one new faction or faction depth]
  → 新干员档案 adds: [2–3 world geography facts, 1 historical reference, implied connections to existing lore]
  → 活动剧情 explores: [Deep-dive on one character or faction, often retcons or enriches Tier 1 material]
  → 隐藏收集品 seeds: [1–3 fragments that pay off in patch 3–6 versions later]
```

The key discipline: **never explain everything in one patch**. Explanation kills demand. A partially answered mystery creates more engagement than a fully closed one.

### Beginner pitfall for game worldbuilding
**Front-loading lore in dialogue:** NPC gives a 3-minute speech explaining world history before the player gets to do anything. Players skip it. All that lore is wasted. **The fix:** deliver lore through *player action* — let them *discover* documents, *witness* consequences, *choose* sides. Information the player earns is information the player remembers.

## Summary — Actionable Checklist

**Before writing Scene 1:**
- [ ] Fill the 6-field Worldview Canvas
- [ ] Choose hard or soft magic — commit to one, know why
- [ ] Run the derivation chain from your Core Lie: physical → economic → social → political → religious → cultural
- [ ] Identify which 2–3 world dimensions your plot *directly* touches

**During writing:**
- [ ] For every lore fact you want to share: can a character's *action, behavior, or visible consequence* carry it instead of an explanation?
- [ ] After every worldbuilding session: does this fact affect a character decision or plot obstacle? If no — stop, write instead.
- [ ] If you add a new magic rule: run the Second Law stress test — what are the costs? What can it *not* do?

**For 二游 game writing:**
- [ ] Define your 3-tier lore depth plan before writing any content
- [ ] Per patch: plant 1–3 Tier 3 seeds that pay off 3–6 patches later
- [ ] Test every lore delivery channel: are you rewarding the casual player *and* the hardcore one?
- [ ] No scene-stopping infodumps — move lore into item descriptions, NPC behavior, architecture, in-world documents
