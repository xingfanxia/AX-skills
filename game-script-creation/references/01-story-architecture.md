## 1. Premise, Theme, and Controlling Idea

### 1.1 What they are (and why beginners confuse them)

| Term | One-line definition | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Concept / Premise** | The "what if" that generates the story | Generative — what starts you |
| **Theme** | A recurring question or tension the story explores | Broad — can be plural, can stay unresolved |
| **Controlling Idea** | A single sentence: *how* and *why* life changes from beginning to end | Definitive — the irreducible meaning of THIS story |

McKee (*Story*, 1997): "A true theme is not a word but a sentence — one clear, coherent sentence expressing a story's irreducible meaning." The controlling idea combines a **value at stake** (love, justice, survival, identity) with the **cause** that drives its change (positive or negative).

**Format:** `[Value] is [won/lost/maintained] because [reason].`
- *Chinatown*: "Justice is destroyed because self-interest corrupts the very institutions meant to protect us."
- *The Matrix*: "Freedom is won because belief in one's own power breaks systems of control."
- Genshin Impact (Sumeru arc, thematically): "Wisdom becomes liberation only when it is no longer forced on others."

### 1.2 Why the controlling idea is the spine

Every story decision — what to keep, what to cut, which scene to write next — can be tested against it: *"Does this support or productively complicate the controlling idea?"*

Without it, writers add cool scenes that feel disconnected. With it, even a single line of dialogue can carry the whole story's weight.

### 1.3 Theme vs. controlling idea in game contexts

Games often explore MULTIPLE themes across a long story (Arknights carries Infection/discrimination, environmentalism, colonialism, and sacrifice across years of updates). That is fine — themes are exploratory. But **each arc or chapter still needs its own controlling idea** to feel complete. A single arc that tries to "do" three themes without a controlling idea will feel unfocused and emotionally unresolved.

**Actionable rule for 二游:** Each version's main story update (e.g., a Chapter or Act) should have its own controlling idea, even if it serves a longer-arc theme. This is how Honkai: Star Rail's individual planet arcs (Jarilo-VI, Luofu, Penacony, Amphoreus) feel satisfying independently while contributing to the series' overarching meditation on memory and entropy.

### 1.4 Extracting a controlling idea from a vague idea

Ask in this order:
1. "What changes by the end?" (What is the protagonist's situation before vs. after?)
2. "Why does it change?" (What force or truth caused the change?)
3. "Is that change a gain or a loss?" (Positive arc, negative arc, or flat arc?)

Draft a sentence using the format. If you cannot, the story does not have a spine yet.

### 1.5 Beginner pitfall: theme as a noun

Writers say "my game is about *loyalty*." That is not a controlling idea — it is a subject. Ask: "What does your story *say* about loyalty?" That answer is the controlling idea. A noun is a theme; a sentence is a controlling idea.

## 2. Structure Frameworks

### 2.1 Classic Three-Act Structure

**What it is:** The bedrock of Western narrative. Syd Field codified it for screenwriting (1979). Origins trace to Aristotle's *Poetics* (beginning / middle / end).

| Act | Proportion | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Act 1 (Setup) | ~25% | Establish world, protagonist, flaw/need. End with Inciting Incident + First Turning Point (protagonist commits to the central goal) |
| Act 2 (Confrontation) | ~50% | Rising obstacles, escalating stakes. Midpoint flips the situation. Ends with Dark Night / All Is Lost moment + Second Turning Point |
| Act 3 (Resolution) | ~25% | Climax (protagonist confronts core conflict), transformation, denouement |

**Famous example:** *Jurassic Park* — Act 1 ends when the park loses power; Act 2 escalates with every escape attempt failing; Act 3 resolves with the T-Rex saving the humans.

**When to use:** Virtually all Western linear game main stories. Default framework to reach for first.

**Game deviation:** In long-running games (live-service gacha, MMORPGs), Act 2 can span MONTHS of updates. Each individual version/patch still needs a mini three-act within it or players disengage. Think of it as **fractal three-act**: macro (entire game), meso (major arc), micro (single chapter/event).

**Beginner pitfall:** Making Act 2 a series of disconnected adventures with no escalating pressure. Every obstacle in Act 2 should cost the protagonist something and raise the stakes — not just delay them.

### 2.2 Freytag's Pyramid (5-Act)

**What it is:** German playwright Gustav Freytag (1863) mapped classical and Shakespearean drama as a five-stage arc. More granular than three-act; useful for understanding the *shape* of tension.

| Stage | Function |
|---|---|
| **Exposition** | World, characters, backstory established |
| **Rising Action** | Complications, antagonist enters, conflict intensifies |
| **Climax** | Turning point — highest tension, outcome determined |
| **Falling Action** | Aftermath, remaining threads unravel |
| **Dénouement** | Resolution, new equilibrium |

**Famous example:** *Macbeth* — Exposition: Macbeth returns from war; Rising Action: witches' prophecy, murder of Duncan; Climax: Macbeth crowns himself; Falling Action: paranoia, Banquo's ghost; Dénouement: death and Malcolm's restoration.

**When to use:** Analyzing existing stories to understand pacing. As a writing framework, three-act is usually more practical.

**Beginner pitfall:** Conflating Climax with the story's most action-heavy scene. The Climax is the *decision point* — the moment the outcome becomes inevitable. Action is a vehicle.

---

### 2.3 Hero's Journey (Campbell) & Vogler's 12-Stage Adaptation

**What it is:** Joseph Campbell (*The Hero with a Thousand Faces*, 1949) identified a universal mythological pattern (the monomyth) across cultures: 17 stages. Christopher Vogler compressed this into a practical 12-stage screenplay guide (*The Writer's Journey*, 1992).

**Vogler's 12 stages:**

1. Ordinary World
2. Call to Adventure
3. Refusal of the Call
4. Meeting the Mentor
5. Crossing the First Threshold
6. Tests, Allies, and Enemies
7. Approach to the Inmost Cave
8. Ordeal
9. Reward (Seizing the Sword)
10. The Road Back
11. Resurrection
12. Return with the Elixir

**Famous example:** *Star Wars: A New Hope* — textbook alignment with all 12 stages (Luke's farm = Ordinary World; Obi-Wan = Mentor; Death Star assault = Ordeal; destruction of Death Star = Return with the Elixir).

**When to use:** RPGs with a chosen-one protagonist; games where a lone hero physically travels through the world; epic fantasy arcs with departure–initiation–return structure. Very strong for 二游 主角 (Trailblazer in HSR, Traveler in Genshin) who are literally wandering outsiders entering new worlds.

**Game deviation:** In gacha games, the "Return with the Elixir" is often ABSENT in the traditional sense — the protagonist rarely returns home, because the game continues. Instead, the *elixir* is shared with the world they visited (the recurring beats of each planetary arc in HSR). Design for local resolution, not global return.

**Beginner pitfall:** Treating the Hero's Journey as a checklist. Campbell's stages are observed patterns, not prescriptions. Skip stages that don't serve the story; combine others. The *spirit* is departure → transformation → return-changed.

### 2.4 Blake Snyder's "Save the Cat" — 15 Beats

**What it is:** Blake Snyder (*Save the Cat!*, 2005) refined three-act into 15 named beats with page/percentage positions. Highly actionable for beginners; criticized by some for over-formulaic application.

**The 15 beats (with approximate position):**

| Beat | Position | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Opening Image | 1% | "Before" snapshot of protagonist's world |
| Theme Stated | ~5% | Another character voices the story's question to the protagonist (they don't hear it yet) |
| Setup | 1–10% | Establish all facets of the protagonist's flawed status quo |
| Catalyst | ~10% | Inciting incident — the world changes, no going back |
| Debate | 10–20% | Protagonist resists the call; internal conflict visible |
| Break into Two | ~20% | Protagonist makes a choice; enters the "upside-down" world |
| B Story | ~22% | New character or relationship enters, carrying the theme |
| Fun and Games | 20–50% | The promise of the premise — protagonist explores the new world |
| Midpoint | 50% | False victory or false defeat; stakes flip |
| Bad Guys Close In | 50–75% | Protagonist's situation worsens on all fronts |
| All Is Lost | ~75% | "Whiff of death" — something or someone dies (physically or symbolically) |
| Dark Night of the Soul | 75–80% | Protagonist hits rock bottom; mourns what is lost |
| Break into Three | ~80% | New insight (often from B Story) gives protagonist a path forward |
| Finale | 80–99% | Protagonist applies the lesson; confronts the antagonist with new strength |
| Final Image | 99% | "After" snapshot — mirrors Opening Image, shows transformation |

**Famous example:** *The Hunger Games* — Opening Image: starving Katniss in District 12; Theme Stated: Peeta's bread memory; Catalyst: Prim's name drawn at the Reaping; Break into Two: Katniss volunteers; Fun and Games: training, Capitol spectacle; Midpoint: public declaration of love strategy; All Is Lost: Rue's death; Break into Three: berries gambit; Final Image: Katniss and Peeta on train.

**When to use:** Excellent for beginners writing a standalone arc or a single event story in a game. The "Theme Stated" beat is gold — it forces you to know your controlling idea *before* you write the middle.

**Game deviation:** "Fun and Games" is uniquely expandable in games — this is where gameplay mechanics and the world's hook live. A game's Fun and Games zone can be 30–40% of total content (exploration, side quests, character introductions) without losing narrative momentum, as long as the Midpoint still flips the stakes sharply.

**Beginner pitfall:** Writing Theme Stated as a direct lecture from one character to another. It must be organic dialogue. The protagonist should *not* understand it yet — the theme is stated to them, not by them.

### 2.5 Dan Harmon's Story Circle (8 Steps)

**What it is:** Harmon (*Rick and Morty*, *Community*) developed his "Story Circle" in the late 1990s, drawing on Campbell and Vogler. Published on channel101.fandom.com as "Story Structure 101: Super Basic Shit." The circle emphasizes that a story is about a character going out of comfort, transforming, and returning changed.

**The 8 steps:**

1. **You** — A character in a zone of comfort
2. **Need** — But they want something
3. **Go** — They enter an unfamiliar situation
4. **Search** — Adapt to it (try/fail cycles)
5. **Find** — Get what they wanted
6. **Take** — Pay a heavy price for it
7. **Return** — Back to familiar situation
8. **Change** — Having fundamentally changed

**Famous example:** Every *Rick and Morty* episode and arc. Also maps cleanly to *Parasite*: Ki-woo (You, Need) → infiltrates the Park household (Go, Search) → fully embedded (Find) → the basement revelation (Take) → chaos/return (Return, Change).

**When to use:** Episodic content (game events, chapters, one-shots). The circle's explicit "Take / Pay a price" beat is crucial — without cost, the story feels consequence-free and shallow.

**二游 application:** Each gacha character's story quest (邀约事件, bond episode, character story) maps almost perfectly to the Story Circle. The character's personal "Need" drives the episode; the "Take / Price" beat is where the character is revealed to be more complex than their surface archetype.

**Beginner pitfall:** Making Step 8 (Change) too complete. Harmon's characters often return changed in ambiguous or incomplete ways — they gained something but lost something else. Perfect redemption arcs feel dishonest.

### 2.6 Dan Wells' 7-Point Story Structure

**What it is:** Author Dan Wells (presented at conventions, video series on YouTube: "Dan Wells on Story Structure") adapted this from the *Star Trek Roleplaying Game Narrator's Guide*. Designed specifically for novelists. Build it **backwards from the Resolution**.

**The 7 points:**

1. **Resolution** — The end state (define this first)
2. **Hook** — The opposite starting state
3. **Plot Turn 1** — The inciting event that sets the protagonist in motion
4. **Pinch 1** — First external pressure applied; protagonist forced to engage
5. **Midpoint** — Protagonist shifts from reactive to proactive; takes charge
6. **Pinch 2** — Maximum pressure; loss of a resource, ally, or option; feels hopeless
7. **Plot Turn 2** — Final piece of information/insight that enables the resolution

**Famous example:** Wells uses *Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone* in his video: Resolution (Harry learns he's a wizard and defeats Voldemort) → Hook (Harry is powerless orphan) → Plot Turn 1 (Hagrid arrives) → Pinch 1 (Troll, Voldemort's presence revealed) → Midpoint (Harry decides to find the Stone himself) → Pinch 2 (Snape/Quirrell confrontation, friends fall) → Plot Turn 2 (Love magic protection revealed).

**When to use:** Plotting a story backwards from a defined ending. Excellent for tightly plotted, single-arc stories. Strong for game main stories where the writers know the ending.

**Game deviation:** In live-service games with no fixed ending, 7-point works best applied at the **arc level**, not the global level. Each major arc gets its own Resolution defined before scripting begins.

**Beginner pitfall:** Not defining the Resolution first. Without a defined end state, writers cannot build the Hook (the opposite of the end). Wells' method only works backwards.

### 2.7 起承転合 / Kishōtenketsu (4-Act, Conflict-Optional)

**What it is:** Classical East Asian narrative structure originating in Chinese four-line poetry (*qǐ-chéng-zhuǎn-hé* in Mandarin; *ki-shō-ten-ketsu* in Japanese). Not well understood in the West — it does NOT require a driving conflict.

**The 4 acts:**

| Act | Name | Function |
|---|---|---|
| 起 (Ki/Qǐ) | Introduction | Establish characters, setting, the "given world" |
| 承 (Shō/Chéng) | Development | Deepen the world; develop characters and relationships; no conflict necessary |
| 転 (Ten/Zhuǎn) | Twist / Turn | An unexpected shift that recontextualizes everything before it |
| 結 (Ketsu/Hé) | Conclusion | Synthesis — the new understanding that results from the Twist |

**The key distinction:** In Western three-act structure, the story is pulled forward by a *driving conflict* that the protagonist must resolve. In kishōtenketsu, the story is pulled forward by a *surprising revelation or recontextualization* (the Ten/転). The audience moves from "I understand this world" to "Oh — I was seeing it wrong."

**Famous examples:**
- Most of Hayao Miyazaki's films (*My Neighbor Totoro* has minimal conflict; the Twist is that Catbus/Totoro reveals a spirit world intersecting with ordinary life)
- *Parasite* (Bong Joon-ho) uses kishōtenketsu structurally — the Ten beat is the revelation of the underground bunker
- Nintendo game design (Shigeru Miyamoto has cited this structure): Super Mario Galaxy's level design introduces a mechanic (Ki), explores it (Shō), subverts it (Ten), and resolves cleverly (Ketsu)

**When to use:**
- Slice-of-life game content (daily life events, character interactions in 二游)
- Stories emphasizing emotional atmosphere over plot
- Character bonding episodes (絆, 好感度, 邀约) where conflict would feel forced
- Japanese-style visual novel segments

**Comparison with Western structure:**

| Dimension | Western 3-Act | Kishōtenketsu |
|---|---|---|
| Engine | Conflict and resolution | Revelation and recontextualization |
| Stakes | Usually explicit (life/death/love) | Can be entirely emotional/perceptual |
| Protagonist need | External goal + internal flaw to overcome | Can be absent or entirely internal |
| Tension source | Antagonism, obstacles | Curiosity and anticipation of the Twist |

**二游 application:** Kishōtenketsu is the **default structure for character story episodes** in JP/KR games (Blue Archive, Uma Musume, PriConne). The episode is not "will the character succeed?" but "what will we discover about this character's inner world?" The Ten beat is the revelation that cracks the character's surface.

**Beginner pitfall (Western writer):** Trying to add conflict to a kishōtenketsu episode because "nothing is happening." Kishōtenketsu builds tension through accumulation of understanding, not through obstruction. Forcing conflict collapses the structure.

**Beginner pitfall (East Asian writer):** Skipping the Ten beat entirely and writing pure slice-of-life with no recontextualization. A kishōtenketsu without a genuine Twist is just four paragraphs of setup.

### 2.8 Framework Selection Guide

| Story shape | Best framework |
|---|---|
| Standalone arc or single game chapter | Save the Cat 15 beats OR 3-act |
| Epic fantasy / chosen-one RPG | Hero's Journey (Vogler) |
| Character-focused episode in gacha | Story Circle OR Kishōtenketsu |
| Tightly plotted thriller/mystery arc | 7-point (build backwards from resolution) |
| Serialized updates with episodic feel | Fractal 3-act (macro + micro) |
| Slice-of-life / low-conflict 二游 content | Kishōtenketsu |
| Analyzing WHY a story's pacing works | Freytag's Pyramid |

Do not force a single framework onto an entire long-form game. Different frameworks serve different scales and content types within the same project.

## 3. Conflict, Stakes, Tension, and Pacing

### 3.1 Types of Conflict

Narrative conflict is not merely "two people arguing." It is a force impeding a character's pursuit of their goal.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Character vs. Character** | External antagonist or rival | Genshin: Traveler vs. Tsaritsa's forces |
| **Character vs. Self** | Internal psychological conflict; opposing desires/beliefs | HSR Trailblazer: existential identity in a universe of Stellaron threats |
| **Character vs. Society** | Protagonist against institutions, norms, systems | Arknights: Infected vs. Oripathy discrimination, colonial governance |
| **Character vs. Nature** | Survival against environment | Any apocalyptic game world |
| **Character vs. Fate/Destiny** | Protagonist challenging or accepting predetermined outcome | FGO: humanity vs. incineration by Goetia |
| **Character vs. Technology/System** | Protagonist trapped or harmed by constructed systems | Cyberpunk narratives; AI antagonists |

**Most powerful stories layer multiple types.** Arknights' Reunion arc combines Character vs. Society (Rhodes Island vs. Columbian government), Character vs. Self (Amiya's wolf nature), and Character vs. Fate (Frostnova's terminal illness).

### 3.2 Stakes and Raising Them

Stakes answer: "What does the protagonist lose if they fail?" Without clear stakes, conflict produces no tension.

**Layers of stakes (escalate these as the story progresses):**

1. **Personal stakes** — Something the protagonist wants badly (identity, love, safety)
2. **Relational stakes** — Someone the protagonist cares about is threatened
3. **Community stakes** — A group/society is endangered
4. **Existential stakes** — The world, humanity, or existence itself is threatened

The mistake beginners make is jumping to existential stakes immediately. Players/readers feel nothing for the world until they feel something for a specific person. Genshin learned this — players who did not care about Teyvat still cared about Paimon's survival, which is why early arcs focus on local, personal stakes (rescue of Dvalin, Venti's freedom).

**The "but" and "therefore" rule (from Trey Parker / Matt Stone of South Park):** Story beats must connect causally with "but" (complication) or "therefore" (consequence), never "and then." "Character tries X, but Y happens, therefore Z." Remove every scene where the link is only "and then" — it has no causal necessity.

### 3.3 Try-Fail Cycles

A character attempts to reach their goal → fails → learns → tries again → fails differently → learns → final attempt succeeds (or succeeds at a cost). The escalating failure pattern is what builds genuine tension.

**Mechanics:**
- "Yes, and..." — character succeeds, but creates a new problem
- "Yes, but..." — character partially succeeds, but loses something
- "No, and..." — character fails AND things get worse (use sparingly; too many deflates hope)
- "No, but..." — character fails, but gains partial information/advantage

The middle of most game stories suffers because try-fail cycles become "go to location, fight enemy, return." Stakes do not escalate because the protagonist does not *lose* anything meaningful between attempts. Every failure should cost something: a relationship, a resource, a belief.

### 3.4 The Midpoint — Structure's Most Underused Tool

The midpoint (positioned at ~50%) serves three possible functions (pick one):

1. **False Victory:** Protagonist appears to win, but the real problem has just begun
2. **False Defeat:** Protagonist loses a major battle, forcing a strategic reinvention
3. **Revelation:** A truth is revealed that recontextualizes the entire story so far

After the midpoint, the protagonist must shift from **reactive** (responding to circumstances) to **proactive** (actively pursuing the solution). This shift is the emotional engine of Act 2's second half.

**Game context:** In chapter-based games, the midpoint chapter of an arc should be the one that makes the player say "oh no." Not the final chapter. The final chapter is where the protagonist wins or loses with full conviction. The midpoint is where the situation flips and the player leans forward.

### 3.5 Setup and Payoff

Every significant payoff must be set up earlier. The setup plants information naturally; the payoff uses that information in a surprising but inevitable way.

**The rule:** Payoffs must feel surprising AND inevitable in hindsight. If the audience says "that came from nowhere," the setup failed. If they say "I knew that was coming," the setup was too obvious.

**In 二游:** A character's backstory (often delivered in their gacha pull screen, profile, or bond episode) is setup. Their climactic story action in the main story is the payoff. Writers who script main story without knowing the character's bond episodes create payoffs with no setups — the emotional resonance is lost on players who invested in the character.

### 3.6 Pacing

Pacing governs the *rhythm* of tension and release.

**Core principle:** Tension requires release to be sustainable. Unrelenting intensity numbs the audience. Use quiet scenes (character bonding, exploration, humor) to create contrast that makes the next tense scene feel urgent.

**Escalation pattern:** Each cycle of tension should reach a higher peak than the last. If scene 3 is more tense than scene 7, the story is decelerating — a structural problem.

**Dialogue pacing:** Short sentences and fragments accelerate pace. Long, complex sentences slow it. During action or confrontation, write short. During reflection or revelation, allow longer rhythms.

**Game-specific pacing note:** Player agency disrupts authorial pacing. Players can pause, backtrack, and speed-read cutscenes. Design for the minimum viable emotional arc — assume a distracted player. Key emotional beats must be in cutscenes or voiced dialogue, not buried in optional text logs. This is a fundamental departure from novels and film.

## 4. The Vague Idea → Logline → Premise → Spine Procedure

This is the core diagnostic workflow for getting a writer from "I have a cool vibe" to a workable story structure.

### 4.1 The Core Sequence

```
VAGUE IDEA / FEELING / IMAGE
        ↓ (§4.2 — Diagnostic questions)
WHAT IT'S REALLY ABOUT (Controlling Idea draft)
        ↓ (§4.3 — Logline construction)
LOGLINE (one sentence)
        ↓ (§4.4 — Structural skeleton)
STRUCTURE SKELETON (choose a framework, fill the turning points)
        ↓
READY TO WRITE
```

### 4.2 Stage 1 — Diagnostic Questions (Vague Idea → Raw Material)

Ask these questions in any order, based on what the user has given you. Record every answer; contradictions are useful.

**On the world / tone:**
- "Describe the feeling or image that started this idea. What does it look like? Sound like?"
- "What genre or tone does it remind you of?" (game, film, book, anime)
- "Who lives in this world? What is their daily life like?"

**On the character:**
- "Who is the story about? What do they want more than anything?"
- "What are they afraid of? What do they believe about themselves or the world that might be wrong?"
- "What would have to happen to change that belief?"

**On the conflict:**
- "What stops the protagonist from getting what they want?"
- "Who or what is the opposing force? Is it a person, a system, nature, themselves?"
- "What is the worst thing that could happen in this story?"

**On the theme:**
- "After reading/playing this story, what do you want the audience to feel or think?"
- "Is there a question you keep asking yourself that the story might be exploring?"
- "What does success look like for your protagonist? What does failure look like?"

### 4.3 Stage 2 — Building the Logline

A logline is one or two sentences. It contains:
- **Who** the protagonist is (with a relevant trait or situation, not just a name)
- **What inciting event** disrupts their world
- **What they want** (external goal)
- **What opposes them** (the central obstacle or antagonist)
- **The stakes** (optional but powerful)

**Template:** "When [inciting incident], [protagonist] must [goal], or else [stakes]."

**Examples:**
- "When a young woman discovers she is the only survivor of a divine massacre, she must infiltrate the empire that ordered it — while hiding the power that makes her the next target."
- "After an amnesiac knight bonds with a world-destroying entity, she must choose between saving the civilization that fears her and accepting the truth of who she was."

**Logline tests:**
- Does it have a clear protagonist?
- Is the conflict clear?
- Does it feel ironic or surprising? (Best loglines have an unexpected twist)
- Is there a time pressure or stakes clause?

**Beginner pitfall:** Writing a premise summary instead of a logline. "A girl goes on an adventure with her friends and learns the value of trust" is a premise summary. A logline creates tension and intrigue in the reader.

### 4.4 Stage 3 — Building the Structural Skeleton

With a logline in hand, select a framework from §2 based on story shape. Then fill in the critical turning points only — not every scene.

**Minimum skeleton (framework-agnostic):**

| Position | Question to answer |
|---|---|
| ~10% | What is the inciting incident? |
| ~20% | What decision does the protagonist make that commits them to the story? |
| ~50% | What happens that makes the situation completely different from before? |
| ~75% | What is the lowest point? What do they lose? |
| ~80% | What insight or resource enables the final attempt? |
| ~95% | What is the climax (final confrontation)? |
| ~100% | What is the world like after the story ends? |

These seven anchors are enough to begin scene-level writing. Every scene must move the story from one anchor toward the next.

### 4.5 Stage 4 — The Controlling Idea Check

Return to §1.4. Now that the skeleton exists, draft a controlling idea sentence:

"In this story, [value] is [gained/lost/preserved] because [reason]."

If you cannot write this sentence, the skeleton is incomplete. A story without a controlling idea will feel thematically hollow even if the plot events are exciting.

**The acid test:** Could you rewrite the ending with the OPPOSITE conclusion (protagonist fails, not succeeds) and the story still feel complete? If yes, the controlling idea is not yet embedded in the structure — it is decorative. The controlling idea must be *proven* or *argued* by the events themselves.

### 4.6 Common Failure Modes and Fixes

| Symptom | Root Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "My middle feels like filler" | No escalating try-fail cycles; stakes do not change | Define what the protagonist LOSES in each failed attempt |
| "My characters feel like chess pieces" | Conflict is external only; characters have no internal contradiction | Give each major character a Want (external) and a Need (internal, opposite or different) |
| "The ending feels unearned" | Climax resolves a conflict that was not set up in Act 1 | Trace back from ending: what must be planted in Act 1 for this ending to feel inevitable? |
| "My theme feels preachy" | Theme is stated directly, not argued through events | Remove all direct statements; let the controlling idea emerge from what happens |
| "My story has good ideas but no plot" | Premise but no controlling idea; no structural skeleton | Run the §4 procedure from scratch with the diagnostic questions |
| "The opening 20 minutes feel slow" | Too much exposition before the Inciting Incident | Move the inciting incident earlier; weave exposition *into* rising action |

## 5. Game-Specific Deviations from Film/Novel Structure

### 5.1 Player Agency Breaks Authorial Pacing

Film controls the pace completely. Games do not. Players may:
- Skip dialogue they find slow
- Grind side content before continuing the main story
- Experience content out of release order (if they joined late)

**Structural implication:** Every major emotional beat must be self-contained enough to function even if the player does not remember a scene from three months ago. Write recaps into dialogue naturally. Do not assume total recall.

### 5.2 Protagonist Design Tension in Gacha

In most story frameworks, the protagonist has a visible flaw and undergoes transformation. Gacha game protagonists (the player avatar — Traveler, Trailblazer, Doctor/Rhodes Island Commander, Arash) are often designed as *flat protagonists* so the player projects onto them. This is intentional but creates a structural problem: the flat protagonist cannot carry a character arc.

**Solution used by successful 二游:** The flat protagonist is the *witness* — they do not transform, but they catalyze transformation in the named gacha characters around them. The named characters ARE the characters with arcs. The protagonist is the Story Circle's "You" who enables other characters to complete their own circles. (This is explicit in Arknights' Doctor role.)

### 5.3 Serialized Release Requires Micro-Arcs

A game updated monthly cannot sustain a single three-act structure across two years without losing the audience. Successful games use **fractal structure**:

- **Macro arc** (years): The overarching controlling idea and three-act spine
- **Meso arc** (major version): A complete kishōtenketsu or three-act that adds to the macro
- **Micro arc** (single chapter/event): A complete Story Circle with its own Need/Take/Change

Each micro arc must be emotionally satisfying standalone while serving the meso and macro. Writers who ignore micro-arc closure produce updates that feel like "nothing happened."

### 5.4 Non-Linear Discovery of Worldbuilding

Games deliver worldbuilding through gameplay (environmental storytelling, item descriptions, optional logs). Unlike novels, players discover context non-linearly. This creates "iceberg" worldbuilding — the story assumes players know LESS than they do, and rewards exploration with deeper layers.

**Structural implication:** Main story scenes should work with *surface-level* knowledge only. The payoff for players who read every in-game book or unlock every hidden audio log is *deeper meaning* — the same events, understood differently. This is a structural technique, not just a writing one.

## Quick Reference: What to Ask at Each Stage

**User has only an image/vibe:**
→ Run §4.2 diagnostic questions. Focus on: "Who is this about? What do they want? What stops them?"

**User has a character but no plot:**
→ Ask: "What does [character] want? What would they have to DO to get it? What's the worst thing that can happen while they try?" → This generates plot.

**User has a plot but no theme:**
→ Ask: "When the story ends, what does the protagonist understand that they didn't at the start? What does THAT understanding say about life?" → Draft a controlling idea.

**User has a premise but the structure feels loose:**
→ Fill in the minimum skeleton from §4.4. The gaps are where the story is missing structure.

**User's middle feels hollow:**
→ Check try-fail cycles (§3.3). Check midpoint function (§3.4). Check that stakes escalate across each attempt.
